## Summary

Bakerlee, Nguyen Ba, Shulgina, Rojas Echenique & Desai (2022) constructed a near-complete combinatorially complete fitness landscape of 10 missense mutations from across the *S. cerevisiae* genome — all $2^{10} = 1024$ genotypes — in six stressful environments, using a hierarchical CRISPR gene drive system that exponentially expands genotype libraries across cross cycles. The paper tests whether apparent **fitness-correlated trends (FCTs)** — mutations having smaller effects in fitter backgrounds, the hallmark of global epistasis — arise from a genuinely non-specific global fitness coupling or from specific idiosyncratic pairwise interactions. By partitioning genotypes by the presence of specific background mutations and by sequentially removing epistatic terms from the inferred model, they show that FCTs are generated by a remarkably small number of idiosyncratic interactions: on average, just 4 pairwise interactions (out of a median 53 total inferred terms) suffice to recapitulate the full FCT, capturing 89% of recoverable variance. This provides experimental validation that apparent global epistasis is a statistical consequence of sparse idiosyncratic interactions rather than a fundamentally non-specific mechanism.

## Conceptual contribution

**Resolution of the global vs. idiosyncratic epistasis dichotomy.** The paper directly addresses a long-standing unresolved debate: whether fitness-correlated trends in adaptation arise from (a) a non-specific global coupling of mutations through background fitness (e.g., from metabolic network topology), or (b) from numerous specific idiosyncratic pairwise interactions whose aggregate statistical pattern mimics global coupling. The discriminating experimental design has two components: (1) *partition test* — if FCTs are global, they should persist within subsets of genotypes defined by the presence or absence of particular background mutations; if idiosyncratic, they should collapse to slope~1 within each partition; (2) *term-removal test* — removing the largest epistatic terms from the inferred model should not collapse an FCT to slope~1 if it is truly global, but should if it is idiosyncratic. Both tests support the idiosyncratic model: FCTs typically vanish when partitioning by 2–3 background loci, and removing just a handful of the largest terms drives the regression slope toward 1. The conclusion is that global and idiosyncratic epistasis are not competing explanations but a unified picture: idiosyncratic interactions generate the phenomenological regularity of global epistasis as a generic statistical consequence when they are sufficiently numerous and scattered.

**Implications for the rule of declining adaptability and evolutionary predictability.** The paper directly connects to Kryazhimskiy2014's rule of declining adaptability. If FCTs arise from idiosyncratic interactions rather than a global mechanism, then fitness remains a useful predictor of future adaptation (because FCTs are real), but the underlying reason is that specific interactions — not a universal fitness-mediated law — happen to produce fitness-correlated summary statistics. This has implications for when the rule will generalize and when it will break down: landscapes where mutations are concentrated in a few functional modules (e.g., antibiotic resistance mutations in efflux or target-alteration pathways) might exhibit stronger idiosyncratic interactions, making FCTs harder to decompose and potentially more robust — or alternatively more environment-specific.

**A new experimental platform for large-scale combinatorial landscape construction.** The hierarchical CRISPR gene drive system is a general platform: by iterating mate-drive-sporulate-select cycles, it builds complete landscapes of up to 16 mutations ($2^{16}$ genotypes) in just 4 cycles. Combined with DNA barcoding for bulk competitive fitness assays, this enables empirical fitness landscapes at a scale previously achievable only by protein-focused deep mutational scanning. The genome-spanning scope is critical because single-protein landscapes exhibit idiosyncratic interactions characteristic of that specific protein's biochemistry; genome-spanning landscapes with diverse functional targets are necessary to study properties relevant to whole-organism adaptation.

## Key findings

- FCTs are common: ~44% of mutations show regression slopes $b \leq 0.9$ or $b \geq 0.9^{-1}$, but not universal: 18/49 mutations with additive effects ≥0.5% show no significant FCT
- FCTs are idiosyncratic, not globally coupled: partitioning the RHO5 G10S FCT (37°C, $b = 0.76$) by WHI2 and AKL1 background genotypes reveals slope~1 within each partition, exposing the specific pairwise interactions generating the trend
- Term-removal analysis confirms: on average, just **4 idiosyncratic interactions** (primarily pairwise) out of a median of 53 total inferred terms suffice to recapitulate the full FCT regression slope within 0.01 — capturing 89% of FCT variance
- Additive and pairwise epistatic terms explain the majority of fitness variance; third-order terms contribute minor amounts; higher orders are negligible — consistent with earlier empirical studies
- Epistatic terms are highly skewed: a small number of terms explain disproportionate variance
- Fitness landscapes are substantially environment-specific: low between-environment correlations of both genotype fitnesses and pairwise interactions across 6 environments
- Haploid and homozygous diploid landscapes are largely correlated, with notable exceptions in the suloctidil environment
- 875/1024 genotypes (85.4%) confirmed after gene drive construction; 407/1024 present in both biological replicates; whole-genome sequencing of 96 strains confirmed rare aneuploidies and spurious mutations unlikely to affect results

## Methods

- **Hierarchical CRISPR gene drive system:** Cre-Lox recombination links guide-RNA (gRNA) arrays linking flanking Lox sites; Cas9 gene drive makes heterozygous diploids homozygous at targeted loci with ≥95% efficiency; each cross cycle doubles the number of mutations incorporated; 4 cycles produce a 10-mutation complete landscape
- **Mutations studied:** 10 missense mutations in 10 genes across 8 chromosomes — AKL1 (S176P), BUL2 (L883F), FAS1 (G588A), MKT1 (D30G), NCS2 (H71L), PMA1 (S234C), RHO5 (G10S), RPI1 (E102D), SCH9 (P220S), WHI2 (L262S) — selected from natural variation and experimental evolution datasets to maximize fitness variance while minimizing pathway-specific epistasis
- **Bulk fitness assays:** DNA-barcoded pooled haploid and homozygous diploid libraries in 6 environments; 7 generations per passage; barcode sequencing at generations 7, 14, 28, 42, 49; relative fitness inferred from barcode frequency changes
- **Epistasis inference:** LASSO regularization to infer additive and epistatic coefficients from fitness data
- **FCT analysis:** regression slope $b$ of $\phi_\text{Mut}$ vs. $\phi_\text{WT}$ across all genotype backgrounds for each focal mutation; weighted total least-squares; partition analysis by background genotype subsets; sequential term-removal analysis

## Open questions raised

- Does the ~4-interaction explanation for FCTs generalize to antibiotic resistance landscapes, where mutations may be concentrated in a few functional modules (efflux, target alteration, membrane permeability)? Pathway-concentrated mutations might exhibit stronger or more numerous idiosyncratic interactions per mutation, potentially producing FCTs that require more terms to decompose — or, conversely, more easily partitioned FCTs if the relevant interacting mutations are known.
- The landscapes here span 6 diverse stressful environments with low between-environment correlations; do antibiotic resistance landscapes show similar or higher between-drug correlations, and does this predict whether FCTs will be more or less environment-specific in resistance contexts?
- The CRISPR gene drive platform can in principle be extended to 16 mutations ($2^{16}$ genotypes); what new questions become answerable at that scale — in particular, does the ~4-interaction rule for FCTs hold at larger landscape sizes, or do higher-order interactions start contributing to FCTs at greater mutation counts?
