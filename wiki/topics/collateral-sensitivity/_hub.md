# Collateral Sensitivity

## What this topic is about

Collateral sensitivity refers to the phenomenon where a population that has evolved resistance to one drug becomes more sensitive — not just equally sensitive — to a different drug. This is distinct from cross-resistance, where resistance to one drug confers resistance to another. Collateral sensitivity arises because the mutations that confer resistance often impose fitness costs or alter cellular physiology in ways that create new vulnerabilities.

The foundational systematic mapping of CS as a network phenomenon was established by [[papers/Lazar2013_AntibioticHypersensitivity|Lazar2013]], which evolved 240 parallel *E. coli* populations across 24 antibiotics and showed that CS is common (at least 35% of antibiotic pairs), non-random (absent between same-target pairs), and structurally explicable: aminoglycoside resistance mutations reduce the proton-motive force (PMF), which simultaneously decreases aminoglycoside uptake (conferring resistance) and diminishes efflux pump activity (conferring hypersensitivity to efflux substrates). This establishes a structural principle: CS between mechanistically disparate antibiotics arises when resistance mutations alter a cellular property exploited in *opposite directions* by different drug classes.

The first demonstration that CS could be systematically exploited as a clinical treatment strategy came from [[papers/Imamovic2013_CollateralSensitivityCycling|Imamovic2013]], which named the concept **collateral sensitivity cycling** and provided its experimental proof of principle. By evolving *E. coli* MG1655 to resistance against 23 antibiotics across 11 drug classes and mapping the resulting CS network, the paper enumerated 207 possible CS cycles and demonstrated that sequential cycling of gentamicin and cefuroxime — chosen for their reciprocal CS profiles — completely eradicated each resistant subpopulation at each drug switch, fully reproducibly. Critically, this mechanism operates through **active selection against resistance** (CS kills resistant cells faster) rather than the passive dilution during drug withdrawal that motivates conventional fitness-cost-based cycling. CS also reduces the mutant prevention concentration (MPC) for resistant bacteria against the CS-target drug, meaning less drug is needed to prevent de novo resistance evolution in an already-resistant population. This paper is the conceptual starting point for all subsequent CS-based evolutionary control strategies.

The central question in this area is whether collateral effects are predictable and exploitable. A key conceptual resolution introduced by [[papers/Maltas2019a_CollateralSensitivity|Maltas2019a]] is that individual-level unpredictability and population-level tractability can coexist: collateral profiles are highly heterogeneous across independently evolved mutants, yet they cluster into statistically coherent groups defined by drug class. This reframes the problem from predicting which specific mutant will be collaterally sensitive to characterizing the *distribution* of collateral effects well enough to design rational policies — making the stochasticity of individual evolutionary outcomes an input to a probabilistic control framework rather than an obstacle.

A complementary extension, introduced by [[papers/Maltas2019b_NonantibioticStressors|Maltas2019b]], is that collateral effects are not confined to antibiotic-antibiotic pairs — they are a general feature of bacterial adaptation spanning chemically diverse stressors, including biocides, preservatives, extreme pH, and osmotic stress. Collateral sensitivity to antibiotics is common even after selection by entirely non-drug agents: more than half (58%) of collateral effects in nonantibiotic-selected *E. faecalis* populations are sensitivities rather than resistances. Crucially, because different nonantibiotic stressors sensitize bacteria to different antibiotics, sequential exposure to orthogonally sensitizing environments can compound vulnerabilities beyond what any single stressor achieves. This raises the possibility that nonantibiotic agents in the patient environment — hand sanitizers, food preservatives, hospital disinfectants — could be leveraged (or avoided) as evolutionary levers.

A formal theoretical bridge between collateral effects and drug interactions is provided by [[papers/Gjini2021_PriceEquationMultidrug|Gjini2021]], which shows that collateral effects constrain evolutionary trajectories by biasing the direction of adaptation on the two-drug fitness landscape. A key implication is that **collateral sensitivity alone does not guarantee slow adaptation** — the fitness landscape gradient can overwhelm the collateral constraint — meaning that exploiting CS optimally requires knowing both the collateral structure of available mutants and the pharmacological interaction between drugs at the relevant dosages.

A closely related extension, introduced by [[papers/Dean2020_DrugComboEvolution|Dean2020]], shows that **simultaneous two-drug environments modulate and can eliminate the collateral effects that single-drug selection produces**. When *E. faecalis* evolves resistance to CRO alone, the resulting population shows CIP collateral sensitivity; when it evolves resistance to CIP alone, it shows CRO collateral sensitivity. But when populations evolve in a CRO-CIP combination, these collateral sensitivities are absent: the combination selects for resistance profiles that avoid the collateral costs that single-drug selection imposes. Similarly, combinations can shift resistance profiles away from the endpoints that produce maximal collateral effects and toward intermediate profiles that balance the two. This means that the collateral landscape — not just the resistance level — depends on the pharmacological context in which resistance evolves, with implications for how collateral effects can be exploited in sequential (post-combination) treatment protocols.

A fourth conceptual layer, introduced by [[papers/Maltas2025_DynamicCollateralSensitivity|Maltas2025]], is that even the population-level collateral profile is not a fixed object — it evolves over time as resistance accumulates. Early adaptation is dominated by broad collateral resistance (over 91% of instantaneous effects in the first two days of selection), plausibly driven by nonspecific efflux or stress responses that confer low-level multi-drug resistance. As the population is forced to acquire more targeted resistance mutations under escalating drug pressure, the landscape shifts toward collateral sensitivity. This temporal arc creates transient windows during which collateral sensitivity to a second drug is maximized — windows that must be precisely timed to exploit. The implication for treatment design is significant: static characterization of collateral profiles (as in endpoint measurements) captures only a snapshot of a moving target. Optimal drug scheduling requires knowledge of *when* collateral sensitivity occurs, not just *whether* it occurs.

A conceptually important expansion of the CS phenomenon, established by [[papers/Lazar2018_CollateralSensitivityAMPs|Lazar2018]], is that CS extends beyond antibiotic-antibiotic pairs to include collateral effects between conventional antibiotics and antimicrobial peptides (AMPs). Cross-domain CS is *more* prevalent than CS between antibiotics (31% vs. 12% of strain-peptide pairs), is mechanistically grounded in LPS charge modifications caused by canonical antibiotic-resistance mutations, and enables an adjuvant strategy: the AMP PGLA, showing CS in 82% of antibiotic-resistant strains, can re-sensitize resistant bacteria at subinhibitory concentrations and prevent de novo resistance evolution from exceeding clinical breakpoints.

A critical mechanistic question — does CS depend on the specific resistance mechanism? — is addressed by [[papers/Roemhild2020_NitrofurantoinCS|Roemhild2020]], which showed that CS to nitrofurantoin (NIT) is **mechanistically convergent**: three structurally unrelated resistance mutations from different selecting drugs all produce NIT-CS through independent molecular routes (nitroreductase overexpression, increased drug uptake, and a novel **drug-response incompatibility** mechanism in which resistance disables the cell's own protective stress response). Mechanistic convergence makes NIT-CS more robust than mechanism-specific CS pairs because evolutionary diversification of resistance routes cannot escape it.

Two findings from *P. aeruginosa* sharpen the picture of CS generalizability. [[papers/Barbosa2017_AlternativeEvolutionaryPaths|Barbosa2017]] demonstrated that CS network topology is **species-specific** (aminoglycoside resistance drives penicillin CS in PA14, rather than the broad multi-drug hypersensitivity seen in *E. coli*) and that even within a single drug treatment, alternative evolutionary paths in replicate populations can produce qualitatively opposing collateral outcomes — CS in some replicates, cross-resistance in others to the same second drug. The CS landscape is therefore not just organism-specific but **path-specific within a single organism**: treatment design must account for the probability distribution over resistance mechanisms a population might evolve, not just the most likely mechanism.

An important but often overlooked dimension of CS is its **dependence on selection pressure strength**. [[papers/Oz2014_SelectionPressure|Oz2014]] showed that strongly and mildly selected *E. coli* populations reach similar final resistance levels yet produce substantially different CS landscapes, with strong selection yielding more extensive CS — adding dose history as an independent axis shaping the collateral profile alongside the identity of the selecting drug, the timepoint of measurement ([[papers/Maltas2025_DynamicCollateralSensitivity|Maltas2025]]), and the species ([[papers/Barbosa2017_AlternativeEvolutionaryPaths|Barbosa2017]]).

A major step toward clinical translation was provided by [[papers/Imamovic2018_PhenotypicConvergence|Imamovic2018]], which studied *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* — the dominant pathogen in cystic fibrosis (CF) chronic lung infections — and established two principles with direct clinical implications. First, **phenotypic convergence**: although resistance can evolve from diverse genetic starting points through diverse mutational routes, the resulting susceptibility phenotypes cluster into a small number of distinct states (4 PCA clusters in *P. aeruginosa*). Clinical DK2 isolates from different CF patients with different host histories independently converge toward these same states upon drug exposure. This dramatically simplifies the effective complexity of the CS landscape: rather than requiring full genotypic characterization, treatment design can be based on predicting which convergent phenotypic state a population has entered and what CS vulnerabilities that state implies. Second, **in vivo validation**: an *nfxB*-mutant subpopulation — whose CS vulnerabilities (aminoglycosides, β-lactams, colistin) were predicted from in vitro data — was completely eradicated from a CF patient's lungs during 2-week intensive therapy using those exact drug classes. This constitutes the first demonstration that CS-guided selective pressure operates in a chronic human infection as predicted by laboratory models. The *nfxB* gene (a transcriptional repressor of MexCD-OprJ efflux) was further proposed as a genomic biomarker — its mutations detectable from sputum sequencing — enabling prospective identification of CS-exploitable subpopulations before designing individualized treatment schedules. Additionally, 30-year longitudinal isolates from CF patients show **oscillatory susceptibility dynamics** rather than monotonically increasing resistance, consistent with CS trade-offs cycling populations between phenotypic states under sequential antibiotic exposures.

The **evolutionary stability** of CS — whether the trade-off persists when populations are given evolutionary time against the CS-targeted second drug — was directly tested by [[papers/Barbosa2019_EvolutionaryStability|Barbosa2019]]. Using *P. aeruginosa* PA14 populations that had evolved resistance to drug A (PIT or CAR) with confirmed reciprocal CS to drug B (STR or GEN), clonal lineages were challenged with drug B under four conditions (mild/strong selection × unconstrained/constrained with drug A). Three outcomes were observed: extinction (frequent — the CS constraint holds and populations die), multidrug resistance escape (occasional — populations overcome the double bind), and trade-off maintenance (predominant among survivors — drug B resistance is accompanied by re-sensitization to drug A). Drug order substantially affected the distribution of outcomes, operating through CS effect size, epistasis among adaptive mutations, and fitness costs. The finding that extinction is frequent and trade-off maintenance predominates among survivors establishes that reciprocal CS functions as a genuine evolutionary trap under sequential drug exposure — not merely a transient phenotypic state that evolution rapidly overcomes. The existence of escape nonetheless establishes CS stability as probabilistic rather than absolute: effectiveness depends on drug pair identity, order, and selection pressure conditions.

A critical empirical challenge for translating CS-based therapies from laboratory to clinic is quantified by [[papers/Tandar2026_ClinicalCollateralSensitivity|Tandar2026]]: CS is strikingly rare in clinical surveillance data. Across >5 million MIC measurements from 30 pathogen species and 86 antibiotics, only 3.03% of species-antibiotic pairs show CS (vs. 41.9% showing collateral resistance). Many CS pairs robustly identified under controlled laboratory evolution — including aminoglycoside CS in ciprofloxacin-resistant *P. aeruginosa* — are absent from clinical isolate populations, likely because clinical populations evolve resistance through diverse mechanisms that don't all confer the same CS trade-offs. This introduces a conceptual distinction between **mechanism-linked CS** (arising from one dominant resistance mechanism and consistent across isolates) and **trajectory-dependent CS** (reliable under one evolutionary path but lost under alternatives). For therapeutic application, only the former class is likely to be clinically actionable. Cross-species conservation is the most tractable operational criterion: six CS pairs conserved across four ESKAPEE pathogens — three involving colistin resistance (cefazolin, ampicillin, levofloxacin), mechanistically linked to LPS-layer loss increasing membrane permeability — represent the best-characterized candidates for broad-spectrum clinical exploitation. The clinical analysis thus motivates a more selective view of which CS pairs to pursue, informed by both mechanistic consistency and cross-species prevalence.

A conceptual synthesis from [[papers/Yang2025_CSReview|Yang2025]] identifies three barriers to clinical CS implementation: (1) high variation in CS profiles within a resistance allele class, making it difficult to predict which mutation will dominate but also creating a richer discovery space for strong CS interactions; (2) erosion of CS by compensatory mutations, which accumulate preferentially in cost-mitigating and cost-compounding genes defined by the genetic interaction network around resistance alleles; and (3) absence of CS to any approved antibiotic in many resistant strains. For this third barrier, differential genetic vulnerability mapping provides a complementary approach — asking not "what antibiotic exploits the fitness cost?" but "what genes does the resistant strain specifically depend on for growth?" This defines synthetic lethal targets and extended CS opportunities even in strains with no detectable antibiotic-to-antibiotic CS. A separate conceptual advance from [[papers/Yang2025_CSReview|Yang2025]] is **within-class CS** — arising when mutations at antibiotic binding sites confer resistance to one drug within a class while increasing sensitivity to another member of the same class due to mutually exclusive substrate specificity. This form of CS is argued to be more clinically robust than between-class CS because substrate specificity depends on binding site sequence conservation rather than systemic physiological trade-offs, making it more likely to be cross-species consistent.

## Key papers

- Imamovic2013 mapped the CS network of *E. coli* MG1655 across 23 antibiotics (11 drug classes): 17/23 resistant strains show CS to at least one other drug; aminoglycoside resistance is the broadest CS hub (CS to 14 drugs from multiple classes including β-lactams, polymyxins, macrolides, tetracyclines, fosfomycin, and nitrofurantoin); 207 possible CS cycles were enumerated from the network, including 22 reciprocal two-drug pairs [[papers/Imamovic2013_CollateralSensitivityCycling|Imamovic2013]]

- Imamovic2013 provided the first experimental proof of principle for collateral sensitivity cycling — sequential GEN/CFX treatment based on reciprocal CS completely and reproducibly eradicated each fluorescently labeled resistant subpopulation at each drug switch step; CS also reduced kill time (resistant strains killed faster than WT by CS drug at 16× MICWT) and reduced MPC (resistant strains require lower drug concentrations of CS-target to prevent de novo resistance than WT), establishing CS as a double mechanism against resistance development [[papers/Imamovic2013_CollateralSensitivityCycling|Imamovic2013]]

- Imamovic2013 demonstrated that CS profiles in *E. coli* MG1655 are 94% concordant across two pathogenic clinical isolates (EC3770, EC3856), with NIT-related interactions accounting for most exceptions — suggesting that CS cycling designed from lab-strain profiles is broadly applicable but NIT should be validated per isolate [[papers/Imamovic2013_CollateralSensitivityCycling|Imamovic2013]]

- Imamovic2013 identified β-lactam/quinolone cross-resistance as an explanation for the failure of clinical cycling programs that rotate these two classes — a structural insight derived from the CS network map [[papers/Imamovic2013_CollateralSensitivityCycling|Imamovic2013]]

- Evgrafov2015 demonstrated in *S. aureus* that CS between constituent drugs of a drug combination is a stronger predictor of resistance evolution at clinically relevant (above-MIC) concentrations than drug interaction type: combinations where single-drug resistance creates CS to the partner drug had significantly lower resistance evolution (Mann-Whitney P < 0.05) — extending CS-based combination design principles to above-MIC conditions and a clinically relevant Gram-positive pathogen [[papers/Evgrafov2015_StaphCS|Evgrafov2015]]

- Lazar2013 established the first systematic network map of CS interactions in *E. coli*: at least 35% of antibiotic pairs show CS in at least one direction, CS is absent between same-target antibiotic pairs ($p = 0.013$) and depleted between pairs with overlapping chemogenomic profiles ($p < 10^{-5}$), and aminoglycosides dominate the network (44% of all CS interactions, spanning DNA synthesis, cell-wall synthesis, and non-aminoglycoside protein synthesis classes) [[papers/Lazar2013_AntibioticHypersensitivity|Lazar2013]]

- Lazar2013 identified the PMF-reduction mechanism as the driver of aminoglycoside-induced hypersensitivity: mutations in trkH (found in 64% of sequenced aminoglycoside-adapted clones), cyoB, hemA, and ispA reduce the membrane electrochemical potential, which simultaneously diminishes AcrAB efflux activity and increases intracellular accumulation of non-aminoglycoside antibiotics; 75% of CS-linked antibiotics are AcrAB substrates; AcrAB overexpression rescues sensitivity in wild-type but not in PMF-deficient mutant backgrounds [[papers/Lazar2013_AntibioticHypersensitivity|Lazar2013]]

- Maltas2019a showed that collateral effects are pervasive across 900 mutant–drug combinations in *E. faecalis* V583 (~73% of combinations show statistically significant changes in IC50) but highly heterogeneous, with profiles for parallel populations evolved to the same drug sometimes differing qualitatively [[papers/Maltas2019a_CollateralSensitivity|Maltas2019a]]

- Maltas2019a showed that despite individual-level heterogeneity, collateral sensitivity profiles cluster by drug class: hierarchical clustering partitions 52 evolved mutants into 7 groups corresponding exactly to known drug classes (cell wall inhibitors, tetracyclines, lipopeptides, oxazolidinones, fluoroquinolones, aminocyclitols, antimycobacterials) [[papers/Maltas2019a_CollateralSensitivity|Maltas2019a]]

- Maltas2019a documented that DAP cross-resistance is surprisingly common (64% of all lineages evolved to other drugs), and that LZD selection can drive higher CHL resistance than direct CHL selection by steering populations across a fitness valley [[papers/Maltas2019a_CollateralSensitivity|Maltas2019a]]

- Maltas2019a found a strong, repeatable collateral sensitivity to rifampicin (RIF) when populations are evolved to cell wall synthesis inhibitors — an effect not previously reported in *E. faecalis* [[papers/Maltas2019a_CollateralSensitivity|Maltas2019a]]

- Maltas2019a showed that variability in collateral profiles between parallel replicates correlates with the degree of resistance to the selecting drug (Spearman $\rho = 0.82$, $p < 10^{-3}$ excluding SPT), indicating that some drugs are more reliable for establishing robust cycling protocols than others [[papers/Maltas2019a_CollateralSensitivity|Maltas2019a]]

- Maltas2025 showed that collateral effects are temporally dynamic in *E. faecalis* V583: early adaptation (days 0–2) confers collateral resistance in 91.25% of instantaneous effects, while subsequent 2-day windows show 33–39% collateral resistance and 52–57% collateral sensitivity — a global shift that is statistically significant but does not overcome the initial resistance burst in cumulative terms [[papers/Maltas2025_DynamicCollateralSensitivity|Maltas2025]]

- Maltas2025 showed that collateral profiles at any single time point correctly predict only ~37–41% of profiles at the next time point (2 days later), indicating near-chance predictability at the individual drug-pair level despite the clear global trend [[papers/Maltas2025_DynamicCollateralSensitivity|Maltas2025]]

- Maltas2025 identified time-dependent dosing windows where collateral sensitivity is maximized: in a 14-day CRO-selection experiment, DOX collateral sensitivity peaks during a 6-day window (days 8–13) confirmed by one-way ANOVA and post-hoc pairwise comparisons; switching before or after this window produces different average sensitivities and, in some populations, collateral resistance [[papers/Maltas2025_DynamicCollateralSensitivity|Maltas2025]]

- Maltas2019b showed that collateral sensitivity to antibiotics is common in *E. faecalis* evolved to nonantibiotic stressors (biocides CHX and TCS, preservative NaBz, osmotic stress NaCl/KCl, extreme pH): 58% of collateral effects are sensitivities, not resistances, across 170 condition-mutant combinations [[papers/Maltas2019b_NonantibioticStressors|Maltas2019b]]

- Maltas2019b demonstrated that sequential LZD→NaBz selection induces sensitivity to ~6 conditions on average — significantly more than LZD alone (~4) or NaBz alone (~4) — confirming that orthogonal environmental sequences can compound sensitizing effects; an additive null model successfully identified this pair as optimal and its predicted profiles matched experimental results [[papers/Maltas2019b_NonantibioticStressors|Maltas2019b]]

- Maltas2019b identified mutations in known antibiotic resistance genes in nonantibiotic-selected populations (e.g., marR efflux regulator in osmotic-stress-adapted populations; penicillin-binding protein EF\_1148 in NaBz-adapted populations), providing candidate mechanisms for cross-environment collateral effects while noting that phenotype-genotype relationships are complicated by epistasis and genetic background [[papers/Maltas2019b_NonantibioticStressors|Maltas2019b]]

- Dean2020 showed that collateral sensitivities observed under single-drug selection can be eliminated by combination therapy: CRO-selected populations show CIP collateral sensitivity, and CIP-selected populations show CRO collateral sensitivity, but populations evolved in CRO-CIP combinations display neither collateral effect — the combination selects for profiles that avoid the collateral costs of single-drug resistance [[papers/Dean2020_DrugComboEvolution|Dean2020]]

- Dean2020 showed that resistance profiles selected under antagonistic drug combinations (AMP-STR, CRO-CIP) are nearly growth-optimized along contours in resistance space connecting the profiles of single-drug-selected populations, suggesting that selection efficiently moves populations toward the best accessible collateral trade-off under each dosage condition [[papers/Dean2020_DrugComboEvolution|Dean2020]]

- Gjini2021 formalized collateral effects as the covariance structure $\Sigma$ of scaling parameters, showing that cross-resistance (positively correlated $\alpha, \beta$) biases evolutionary trajectories toward rapid resistance to both drugs simultaneously, while collateral sensitivity (negatively correlated) constrains motion in a direction that slows growth adaptation — the magnitude of this constraint depends multiplicatively on the local geometry of the drug interaction surface and shifts with dosage ratio [[papers/Gjini2021_PriceEquationMultidrug|Gjini2021]]

- Maltas2025 reported that no evolved strain became collaterally sensitive to LZD at any time point, and DAP collateral sensitivity was rare throughout — notable because these are last-resort antibiotics for MDR gram-positive infections and their collateral profiles appear stable across the evolutionary trajectory [[papers/Maltas2025_DynamicCollateralSensitivity|Maltas2025]]

- Lazar2018 showed that 60 antibiotic-resistant *E. coli* strains exhibit CS to antimicrobial peptides at 2.6x the rate of cross-resistance (31% vs. 12% of strain-peptide pairs across 24 AMPs), inverting the pattern seen for antibiotic-antibiotic interactions where cross-resistance is 2-3x more common [[papers/Lazar2018_CollateralSensitivityAMPs|Lazar2018]]

- Lazar2018 identified LPS modification as the mechanistic driver of CS to AMPs: antibiotic-resistance mutations in marR upregulate the kinase WaaY, increasing outer membrane negative surface charge and enhancing cationic AMP killing efficiency — confirmed by zeta potential measurements, qRT-PCR (~3.9-fold waaY upregulation), chemogenomic screen (LPS biosynthesis genes enriched among CAP18-sensitizing genes, odds ratio = 4.4), and genetic epistasis (waaY deletion abolishes marR-mediated CS to P3 peptides) [[papers/Lazar2018_CollateralSensitivityAMPs|Lazar2018]]

- Lazar2018 showed that PGLA (a magainin-family AMP) exhibits CS in 82% of antibiotic-resistant strains; administered at subinhibitory concentrations as an adjuvant, it caused up to 30-fold re-sensitization of laboratory-evolved and clinical resistant *E. coli* strains and prevented de novo resistance evolution from exceeding EUCAST clinical breakpoints over 160 generations of laboratory evolution with ciprofloxacin and tetracycline [[papers/Lazar2018_CollateralSensitivityAMPs|Lazar2018]]

- Roemhild2020 identified three distinct molecular mechanisms by which different antibiotic-resistant backgrounds produce CS to nitrofurantoin: nitroreductase overexpression (NfsA/NfsB upregulated in all three mutants; NfsA critical for initial survival, NfsB for killing rate), increased drug uptake (*hemL* mutant; 2× intracellular NIT accumulation directly measured), and drug-response incompatibility via SOS/SulA (*lon* mutant) — demonstrating that mechanistic convergence on a single CS drug can arise across structurally unrelated resistance mutations through independent pathways [[papers/Roemhild2020_NitrofurantoinCS|Roemhild2020]]

- Roemhild2020 introduced **drug-response incompatibility** as a novel CS mechanism class: resistance mutations that disable Lon protease prevent clearance of the SOS effector SulA, converting a normally transient drug-induced growth arrest into sustained lethality — CS magnitude 16–35× despite *reduced* NIT uptake; confirmed by ΔsulA suppression, LexA1(Ind-) partial rescue, and temperature-dependent ClpYQ-mediated rescue at 43°C; delayed-onset kinetics (divergence 2–5 h post-NIT) operationally distinguish this mechanism from uptake-based CS [[papers/Roemhild2020_NitrofurantoinCS|Roemhild2020]]

- Barbosa2017 established that CS network topology is species-specific in *P. aeruginosa* PA14: aminoglycoside resistance → primarily penicillin CS (not broad multi-drug hypersensitivity as in *E. coli*); CIP resistance → broad hypersensitivity (opposite of *E. coli*); chemical similarity between antibiotics (Jaccard's index) significantly predicts cross-resistance frequency across all drug pairs (Spearman correlation), providing a structure-based criterion for anticipating pleiotropic resistance consequences [[papers/Barbosa2017_AlternativeEvolutionaryPaths|Barbosa2017]]

- Barbosa2017 demonstrated within-treatment variation in collateral phenotypes: among 10 replicate populations evolved to the same drug (e.g., CEF), some show aminoglycoside CS and others show cross-resistance to the same second drug — contrasting outcomes explained by distinct resistance mutations selected in each replicate; this is qualitatively different from the drug-class-level clustering observed in *E. faecalis* and *E. coli* studies and implies that treatment design must account for the distribution of possible resistance mechanisms, not just the modal one [[papers/Barbosa2017_AlternativeEvolutionaryPaths|Barbosa2017]]

- Barbosa2017 validated three regulatory gene mutations as CS determinants in ancestral PA14: *nalC* 500 bp deletion (β-lactam-adapted → aminoglycoside CS; MexAB-OprM regulator); *mexZ* Q95stop (STR-adapted → penicillin CS; MexXY-OprM regulator); *pmrB* P254L and V136E (GEN-adapted → penicillin CS; outer membrane lipid A remodeling); two TetR-like repressors with opposite CS directionality — *nalC* and *mexZ* LOF each produce aminoglycoside resistance but drive CS in opposite directions (aminoglycosides vs. penicillins) likely via pump competition between MexAB-OprM and MexXY-OprM [[papers/Barbosa2017_AlternativeEvolutionaryPaths|Barbosa2017]]

- Imamovic2018 demonstrated that *P. aeruginosa* resistance evolution converges to 4 distinct phenotypic states (PCA clusters) regardless of which drug drove resistance, and that clinical CF isolates from different patients and genetic backgrounds similarly converge toward these states — reducing the effective complexity of the CS landscape from an arbitrary genotype-to-phenotype mapping to prediction from a small number of attractor states [[papers/Imamovic2018_PhenotypicConvergence|Imamovic2018]]

- Imamovic2018 identified *nfxB* mutations as a genetic biomarker for collateral sensitivity in *P. aeruginosa*: *nfxB* encodes the repressor of MexCD-OprJ efflux; loss-of-function mutations upregulate MexC (confirmed by proteomics: $t$-ratio 4.2–6.2, $p < 0.01$) and produce collateral sensitivity to aminoglycosides, β-lactams, and colistin in quinolone-, macrolide-, and tetracycline-resistant strains; *nfxB* mutations consistently co-occur with ciprofloxacin resistance across both PAO1 and all five DK2 clinical isolates tested [[papers/Imamovic2018_PhenotypicConvergence|Imamovic2018]]

- Imamovic2018 provided the first in vivo confirmation that CS-based selective pressure operates in a chronic human infection: an *nfxB*-mutant subpopulation comprising 37.3% of the quinolone-resistant cells before 2-week intensive antibiotic therapy was completely eradicated by the end of treatment (absent in all t2 and t3 isolates) after exposure to aminoglycoside and β-lactam drugs toward which *nfxB* mutants were collaterally sensitive [[papers/Imamovic2018_PhenotypicConvergence|Imamovic2018]]

- Imamovic2018 documented oscillatory susceptibility dynamics in longitudinal *P. aeruginosa* DK2 isolates spanning 30 years of chronic CF infection: rather than monotonically increasing resistance, MIC values fluctuate over decades; patient 333's last isolate (333-2007c) was more susceptible to 14 drugs than the first isolate from 15 years earlier — consistent with CS trade-offs cycling populations between phenotypic states under sequential drug exposures [[papers/Imamovic2018_PhenotypicConvergence|Imamovic2018]]

- Oz2014 established that selection pressure strength (dose during evolution) modulates the CS landscape: strongly selected *E. coli* populations show antibiotic susceptibility (CS) in 10% of cross-measured drug pairs vs. 6% for mildly selected populations ($P = 2 \times 10^{-5}$), and aminoglycoside-resistant strains under strong selection acquire susceptibility to ~6 drug classes vs. ~2 under mild selection — demonstrating that dose history is a determinant of the cross-resistance/CS profile independent of final resistance magnitude [[papers/Oz2014_SelectionPressure|Oz2014]]

- Oz2014 provided independent confirmation that TrkH mutations are a robustly selected outcome of strong aminoglycoside evolution: novel TrkH alleles (L80Q in 4 strains, L153P in 1 strain) across four aminoglycosides (STR, AMK, KAN, TOB) in *E. coli* MG1655 produce widespread susceptibility to distant drug classes, confirming the PMF/efflux antagonism mechanism of [[papers/Lazar2013_AntibioticHypersensitivity|Lazar2013]] with different alleles, strain, and experimental conditions [[papers/Oz2014_SelectionPressure|Oz2014]]

- Barbosa2019 established that reciprocal CS in *P. aeruginosa* is evolutionarily stable under sequential drug exposure: across two-step evolution experiments using PIT↔STR and CAR↔GEN pairs, the most common outcome among lineages that survived the second drug was trade-off maintenance (re-sensitization to drug A upon drug B resistance), with extinction frequent and multidrug resistance escape comparatively rare — providing the first systematic experimental validation that CS functions as a genuine evolutionary trap [[papers/Barbosa2019_EvolutionaryStability|Barbosa2019]]

- Barbosa2019 demonstrated that drug order determines the distribution of evolutionary outcomes (extinction, escape, trade-off maintenance) during CS cycling, operating through at least three mechanisms: the magnitude of the CS effect (how hypersensitive bacteria are to the second drug), epistasis among resistance mutations (whether mutations selected under the second drug are compatible with first-drug resistance mutations), and fitness costs of resistance — establishing drug order as a non-interchangeable design parameter for CS-based sequential therapy [[papers/Barbosa2019_EvolutionaryStability|Barbosa2019]]

- Barbosa2019 showed that constrained environments (maintaining drug A alongside drug B during the second selection step) alter evolutionary dynamics relative to unconstrained conditions — a finding directly relevant to the design of combination-constrained sequential protocols in the clinic [[papers/Barbosa2019_EvolutionaryStability|Barbosa2019]]

- Tandar2026 quantified CS prevalence across 5.1M clinical MIC measurements from 30 pathogen species and 86 antibiotics, finding that CS is rare (3.03% of 12,024 species-antibiotic pairs) relative to collateral resistance (41.9%), and that CS interactions are predominantly species-specific — 220 pairs found in only 1 species, 45 in 2 species — establishing that most lab-identified CS pairs do not produce consistent species-wide patterns in clinical isolate populations [[papers/Tandar2026_ClinicalCollateralSensitivity|Tandar2026]]

- Tandar2026 identified six CS pairs conserved across four ESKAPEE pathogens (*A. baumannii*, *E. coli*, *K. pneumoniae*, *P. aeruginosa*): colistin→cefazolin, colistin→ampicillin, colistin→levofloxacin (all mechanistically linked to LPS-layer loss), linezolid→azithromycin, tobramycin→cefuroxime, and chloramphenicol→amoxicillin-clavulanate; one three-way interaction (linezolid + meropenem → azithromycin) conserved across four Gram-positive species — providing the most concrete current targets for broad-spectrum CS-based therapy [[papers/Tandar2026_ClinicalCollateralSensitivity|Tandar2026]]

- Yang2025 synthesized three barriers to clinical CS implementation: (1) high variation in CS/XR profiles within a resistance allele class (hard to predict which mutation dominates, but implies a richer CS discovery space); (2) erosion of CS by compensatory mutations in cost-mitigating and cost-compounding genes; (3) absence of CS to any clinical antibiotic in many resistant strains — and proposed that high variation is advantageous for discovery of strong, strain-specific CS, while genetic vulnerability mapping addresses the third barrier for strains with no detectable antibiotic-to-antibiotic CS [[papers/Yang2025_CSReview|Yang2025]]

- Yang2025 demonstrated within-class CS from deep mutagenesis of β-lactamase active sites: CTX-M-15 single-amino-acid substitution N135D confers 50-fold MIC increase to mecillinam at the expense of cefotaxime resistance; AmpC (blaampC) mutants with increased aztreonam resistance show CS to piperacillin and cefepime; within-class CS is argued to be more cross-species conserved than between-class CS because it depends on binding site sequence conservation — meaning resistant populations harboring these mutations cannot evolve resistance to both drugs simultaneously [[papers/Yang2025_CSReview|Yang2025]]

## Key concepts and methods

- [[concepts/markov-decision-process|Markov decision process (MDP)]] — framework for designing optimal drug-cycling policies from collateral sensitivity data
- **IC50 measurement via Hill-like dose-response fitting** — $f(x) = (1 + (x/K)^h)^{-1}$; used to quantify resistance as $C \equiv \log_2(\text{IC}_{50,\text{Mut}} / \text{IC}_{50,\text{WT}})$
- **Serial-passage laboratory evolution** — 8-day protocol with daily 200-fold dilutions and escalating drug concentrations; 4 replicate populations per drug
- **Hierarchical clustering on collateral profile vectors** — correlation metric distance; reveals drug-class structure in phenotypic space
- **MAGE-seq (multiplex automated genome engineering + sequencing)** — deep mutagenesis of resistance-determining regions or enzyme active sites for comprehensive CS/XR profiling across all possible single-amino-acid substitutions [[papers/Yang2025_CSReview|Yang2025]]
- **Transposon sequencing (Tn-seq) / CRISPRi** — genome-wide differential fitness mapping to identify cost-mitigating and cost-compounding genes in resistant backgrounds; defines genetic vulnerability targets complementary to CS [[papers/Yang2025_CSReview|Yang2025]]

## Open questions

- ~~How conserved are collateral sensitivity profiles across closely related species or strains?~~ **Addressed by [[papers/Tandar2026_ClinicalCollateralSensitivity|Tandar2026]]**: CS is predominantly species-specific in clinical isolate populations; only 6 pairwise CS interactions are conserved across ≥4 pathogen species. Conservation is rare and mechanistically constrained — most conserved interactions involve colistin resistance (LPS-layer loss). New open question: are there CS interactions in *E. faecalis* clinical isolates that are conserved across *Enterococcus* species (not tested in Tandar2026), and how do they compare to the profiles characterized in laboratory *E. faecalis* V583?
- How much do collateral effects depend on the mechanism of resistance (target modification vs. efflux vs. enzymatic inactivation)? **Partially addressed for nitrofurantoin by [[papers/Roemhild2020_NitrofurantoinCS|Roemhild2020]]**: three structurally unrelated resistance mechanisms (lon, spoT, hemL mutations from tigecycline, mecillinam, and protamine selection) all produce CS to NIT — through different molecular routes — indicating that CS can be mechanism-robust (multiple routes converge on the same target drug) while still mechanism-dependent (route determines magnitude and kinetics). Whether analogous mechanistic convergence exists for other CS drug pairs, and what structural features of a drug make it a convergent CS target, remain open. Genome sequencing identifies candidate genes but cannot resolve mechanisms [[papers/Maltas2019a_CollateralSensitivity|Maltas2019a]].
- ~~Can collateral sensitivity be maintained over multiple rounds of drug cycling, or do populations evolve cross-resistance to all drugs in the cycle?~~ **Partially addressed by [[papers/Maltas2020_TunableFitnessLandscapes|Maltas2020]]**: in the theoretical model of anticorrelated fitness landscapes (mutual collateral sensitivity), rapid alternation drives ergodic-like dynamics that broadly sample genotype space; shared maxima can still emerge under sufficient epistasis, providing a theoretical warning that cycling may produce cross-resistance even with mutual collateral sensitivity. New question: what is the empirical timescale over which shared maxima become the dominant resistance outcome under drug cycling, and can it be predicted from measurable landscape or collateral sensitivity properties? [[papers/Maltas2019a_CollateralSensitivity|Maltas2019a]], [[papers/Maltas2020_TunableFitnessLandscapes|Maltas2020]]
- ~~How do collateral profiles change dynamically as resistance accumulates?~~ **Addressed by [[papers/Maltas2025_DynamicCollateralSensitivity|Maltas2025]]**: early adaptation strongly favors collateral resistance; later stages shift toward sensitivity; but individual drug-pair dynamics are idiosyncratic and near-unpredictable between time steps. New open question: what molecular mechanisms drive this global temporal shift (early efflux/stress-response resistance is a plausible hypothesis but genotypic data are not reported), and how conserved is it across species and drug panels?
- How wide are transient collateral sensitivity windows for drug pairs beyond CRO→DOX, and can window timing and width be predicted from early-timepoint collateral data?
- Do nonantibiotic stressors encountered in clinical settings (biocides, preservatives, osmotic environments) systematically shift bacterial populations toward antibiotic sensitivity, resistance, or neither? [[papers/Maltas2019b_NonantibioticStressors|Maltas2019b]] shows this is measurable in laboratory evolution but clinical relevance needs direct study.
- Can sequential nonantibiotic→antibiotic or nonantibiotic→nonantibiotic selection strategies be extended beyond two steps using the additive model as a guide, and how does epistasis accumulate across longer sequences? [[papers/Maltas2019b_NonantibioticStressors|Maltas2019b]]
- How reliably can the collateral effects produced by single-drug selection be exploited in sequential protocols when prior combination therapy has already eliminated those collateral sensitivities? [[papers/Dean2020_DrugComboEvolution|Dean2020]] shows that combination selection removes the collateral sensitivities that single-drug selection produces, raising the question of whether clinical patients who received combination therapy have a different collateral sensitivity landscape than those who received single drugs.
- In the Price equation framework, the constraining effect of collateral sensitivity on resistance evolution shifts with dosage ratio; can optimal dosages be identified that maximally exploit collateral sensitivity for a given drug pair, and does this criterion align with or contradict MDP-derived cycling policies? [[papers/Gjini2021_PriceEquationMultidrug|Gjini2021]]
- Does epistasis between resistance mutations reshape collateral profiles in ways that undermine the static MDP model? [[papers/Maltas2019a_CollateralSensitivity|Maltas2019a]] A theoretical study of paired fitness landscapes [[papers/Maltas2020_TunableFitnessLandscapes|Maltas2020]] shows that even anticorrelated landscape pairs (the model of mutual collateral sensitivity) can generate shared fitness maxima under sufficient ruggedness, implying that cycling drugs with mutual collateral sensitivity may still produce cross-resistance — a warning that the benefit of collateral sensitivity cycling depends on the epistatic structure of the resistance landscape.

- Does the LPS-modification mechanism of AMP-CS (marR→WaaY→LPS charge) make it more reliably conserved across clinical isolates than antibiotic-antibiotic CS — and can this be leveraged to design a validated AMP-antibiotic cycling protocol? [[papers/Lazar2018_CollateralSensitivityAMPs|Lazar2018]] provides preliminary evidence of reciprocal CS (CAP18-resistant strains are sensitive to multiple antibiotics and vice versa), but conservation was validated for only three uropathogen isolates and full cycling proof-of-principle has not been demonstrated.

- What distinguishes "mechanism-linked CS" (arising from one dominant resistance mechanism, consistent across isolates and detectable clinically) from "trajectory-dependent CS" (reliable under one evolutionary path but absent under alternative clinical resistance routes)? Within-class CS from binding site mutagenesis ([[papers/Yang2025_CSReview|Yang2025]]) may be a concrete instance of mechanism-linked CS — can this distinction be predicted from prior knowledge of resistance mechanism diversity, and does it explain the lab-clinic CS gap documented by [[papers/Tandar2026_ClinicalCollateralSensitivity|Tandar2026]]?

- Does the 14.2% of clinical CS interactions that would shift mean MIC across the susceptibility breakpoint translate to clinical benefit at the individual patient level — does a population-level statistical shift in MIC produce meaningful treatment responses in practice?

- When within-treatment variation produces a mixed population of CS-conferring and cross-resistance-conferring subpopulations, which one wins under cycling treatment — does the cycling protocol selectively deplete the CS subpopulation while enriching the cross-resistant one, and can the outcome be predicted from the frequency distribution of resistance mechanisms? [[papers/Barbosa2017_AlternativeEvolutionaryPaths|Barbosa2017]]

- Does chemical similarity (Jaccard's index) remain a useful predictor of cross-resistance frequency across phylogenetically diverse bacteria, and can it be combined with mechanism-based CS predictions to design drug panels that simultaneously minimize cross-resistance and maximize exploitable CS? [[papers/Barbosa2017_AlternativeEvolutionaryPaths|Barbosa2017]]

- Does within-class CS from β-lactamase binding site mutagenesis (CTX-M-15, AmpC) translate to naturally occurring clinical resistance alleles, or is comprehensive coverage of low-frequency resistance alleles only achievable through engineered mutant libraries? If so, can deep mutagenesis campaigns of binding sites become a routine pre-approval screen for within-class CS before new β-lactam derivatives are introduced? [[papers/Yang2025_CSReview|Yang2025]]

- What is the genetic landscape of secondary mutations that erode CS (the "compensatory mutation landscape") — do cost-mitigating and cost-compounding genes differ systematically between resistance mechanisms, and can this information be used to predict which CS pairs will remain actionable at the time of clinical treatment (after additional evolution has occurred)? [[papers/Yang2025_CSReview|Yang2025]]

- What quantitative properties of a reciprocal CS pair — effect size, symmetry, epistatic compatibility of resistance mutations, fitness costs, drug order — predict the distribution over extinction, escape, and trade-off maintenance during a drug switch? Can these be measured prospectively to rank-order drug pairs by "evolutionary trap" reliability, and can escape probability be explicitly incorporated into MDP/RL policy optimization or mitigated by constrained environments (maintaining both drugs simultaneously)? [[papers/Barbosa2019_EvolutionaryStability|Barbosa2019]]

- Can *nfxB* biomarker-guided CS cycling be validated prospectively in a multi-patient CF clinical trial — and does phenotypic convergence to a small number of attractor states (4 in *P. aeruginosa*) hold across other pathogens and drug panels, or is it specific to the efflux network topology of that organism? Imamovic2018 demonstrates proof-of-concept in a single patient; generalization to other organisms and multi-patient validation remain absent. [[papers/Imamovic2018_PhenotypicConvergence|Imamovic2018]]

- Does selection pressure strength (dose) modulate CS profiles in clinical pathogens (*E. faecalis*, *S. aureus*, *P. aeruginosa*) as it does in *E. coli* — and if so, can dose be incorporated as an explicit evolutionary control variable in treatment design? [[papers/Oz2014_SelectionPressure|Oz2014]] establishes the relationship in *E. coli* lab evolution; clinical generality is untested.
