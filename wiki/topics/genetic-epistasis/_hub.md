# Genetic Epistasis

## What this topic is about

Genetic epistasis refers to interactions between mutations such that the combined fitness effect of two or more mutations differs from what would be expected from their individual effects. When two mutations combine to produce less fitness benefit than expected (negative epistasis), or more (positive epistasis), the evolutionary dynamics of the system are fundamentally altered. Epistasis determines whether mutations are accessible in sequence (whether the second mutation is beneficial given the first), and thus shapes the topology of the fitness landscape and the predictability of evolutionary trajectories.

In the context of antibiotic resistance, epistasis between resistance mutations determines whether multi-drug resistance can evolve incrementally (one mutation at a time) or requires simultaneous acquisition of multiple mutations. Sign epistasis — where the fitness effect of a mutation changes sign depending on genetic background — is particularly important because it creates local fitness peaks and constrains the paths available for adaptation.

The lab is interested in epistasis both as a fundamental property of fitness landscapes and as a practical constraint on evolutionary trajectories under drug treatment. Understanding the epistatic structure of resistance mutations informs predictions about which drug sequences or combinations will limit the evolution of multi-drug resistance.

The original empirical establishment of **global diminishing returns epistasis** as the dominant form of epistasis in *S. cerevisiae* adaptation comes from [[papers/Kryazhimskiy2014_GlobalEpistasis|Kryazhimskiy2014]]. The paper distinguishes three competing models for why lower-fitness genotypes adapt more rapidly — the **rule of declining adaptability**: (1) **modular epistasis** — high-fitness genotypes have fewer beneficial mutation "modules" remaining; (2) **idiosyncratic epistasis** — different genetic backgrounds have different sets of accessible beneficial mutations with widely varying effects; (3) **global epistasis** — all genotypes draw beneficial mutations from the same pool, but each mutation's effect consistently declines with background fitness. The discriminating prediction is that modular and idiosyncratic epistasis require that lines descended from the same Founder share more mutations than lines from different Founders (because beneficial mutations are background-specific), while global epistasis requires no such sharing. Testing this with 104 whole-genome-sequenced clones from 13 Founder backgrounds, lines from the same Founder showed no elevated mutation sharing — ruling out modular and idiosyncratic models. Targeted knockouts of three beneficial genes (*GAT2*, *WHI2*, *SFL1*) across 18 genetic backgrounds confirmed global epistasis directly: fitness effects decline with background fitness with no idiosyncratic genotype-specific deviations. The biological implication is striking: many mutations scattered across functionally unrelated processes (Ras/PKA regulation, cell cycle, filamentous growth) are globally coupled through background fitness alone, implying that the fitness landscape has a lower effective dimensionality than the number of distinct beneficial mutations suggests. Fitness — not specific genotype — is the sufficient statistic for predicting future adaptation.

A critical further advance from [[papers/Ardell2024_GlobalEpistasis|Ardell2024]] is the demonstration that **global epistasis** — the tendency for mutation fitness effects to show diminishing returns in high-fitness backgrounds — is not merely a pattern observed in one environment, but is nearly invariant across environments. Measuring ~100 insertion mutations across 42 *S. cerevisiae* strains in six factorial environments, they show that the global epistasis slope $b_m$ (the rate at which a mutation's effect scales with background genotype growth rate) is statistically indistinguishable across environments for 87% of pairwise comparisons. The environment modulates only one effective parameter: the **pivot growth rate** $\bar{\lambda}_e$, at which an average mutation switches sign. This yields the generalized global epistasis equation:

$$\Delta\lambda_{mge} = b_m(\lambda_{ge} - \bar{\lambda}_e) + \eta_{me} + \xi_{mge}$$

where $b_m$ is environment-invariant, $\bar{\lambda}_e$ is environment-specific, and $\eta_{me}$, $\xi_{mge}$ capture residual unpredictability. The "bowtie" pattern — mutation effects fanning out from the pivot in both directions — implies that DFE variance is minimized when background fitness equals the pivot, and the DFE is predictable across any genotype and environment from just two numbers: background growth rate and the environment's pivot growth rate. This substantially reduces the effective dimensionality of G×G, G×E, and G×G×E interactions.

A mechanistic resolution of the apparent contradiction between idiosyncratic and global epistasis comes from [[papers/Bakerlee2022_IdiosyncraticEpistasis|Bakerlee2022]], which constructed a near-complete combinatorially complete fitness landscape of 10 missense mutations (all $2^{10} = 1024$ genotypes) from diverse functional categories across the *S. cerevisiae* genome in six environments, using a hierarchical CRISPR gene drive system. The central finding is that **fitness-correlated trends (FCTs)** — the regression slope $b$ of $\phi_\text{Mut}$ vs. $\phi_\text{WT}$ deviating from 1, indicating a mutation's effect correlates with background fitness — arise from just ~4 specific idiosyncratic pairwise interactions on average, not from non-specific global fitness coupling. The discriminating partition test: if FCTs are global, they should persist within subsets of genotypes defined by the presence or absence of particular background mutations; if idiosyncratic, they should collapse to slope~1 within each partition. For example, the RHO5 G10S mutation's FCT ($b = 0.76$) at 37°C vanishes when genotypes are partitioned by WHI2 and AKL1 background genotypes, with each partition showing slope~1 — revealing that the apparent global trend was produced by those two specific pairwise interactions. A complementary term-removal analysis confirms: removing just a few of the largest epistatic terms drives the regression slope toward 1, while under a true global model it would not. On average, 4 idiosyncratic interactions (out of a median 53 total inferred terms) suffice to recapitulate 89% of recoverable FCT variance. This provides experimental validation of theoretical predictions that global epistasis is a generic statistical consequence of sufficiently numerous, scattered idiosyncratic interactions — not a reflection of a fundamentally non-specific global mechanism. The key implication for evolutionary prediction is nuanced: fitness remains a useful predictor of adaptability (because FCTs are real and common), but the underlying landscape is not truly low-dimensional; the apparent simplicity is a phenomenological regularity arising from a sparse set of specific interactions that happen to produce fitness-correlated summary statistics across the full genotype space.

An important and distinct form of epistasis is **drug-level epistasis** — the interaction between two drugs' effects on bacterial growth, quantified by the shape of the MIC isobologram. [[papers/Michel2008_MultidrugsResistance|Michel2008]] showed that drug epistasis is a primary determinant of the mutant selection window (MSW) in two-drug combinations: synergistic drug pairs produce larger MSWs than antagonistic pairs (for equivalent cross-resistance), meaning drug-level epistasis directly governs the potential for resistance evolution in combination therapy. The drug-level epistasis parameter $\varepsilon$ (shape of the MIC line) is conceptually distinct from mutation-level epistasis (interaction between two mutations' fitness effects) but both share the same mathematical structure — both describe deviations from additive expectations in their respective domains.

Direct evidence that earlier mutations open or close later adaptive paths — at scale, across many genes and multiple replicate populations — comes from [[papers/Good2017_MolecularEvolutionLTEE|Good2017]], which used metagenomic time series from 12 LTEE *E. coli* populations to establish **historical contingency and potentiation** as general mechanisms of evolutionary contingency rather than curiosities confined to specific innovations. Two statistical analyses build the case. First, gene mutation appearance times are non-randomly distributed over 60,000 generations: some genes (e.g., *hslU*) are mutated predominantly early, while others (e.g., *atoS*) predominantly late — and the global distribution of appearance times is more dispersed than expected by chance (p<0.001), indicating a mixture of early coupon-collecting (abundant targets depleted first) and late potentiation (contingent targets accessible only after earlier mutations). Second, ~16 "missed opportunities" are identified — populations that lacked mutations in specific target genes despite all other populations acquiring them — and the number of such gaps exceeds a random permutation null significantly (p~0.003). These missed opportunities are interpreted as signatures of potentiation: the populations that acquired mutations in these genes possessed earlier mutations that made those paths accessible, while the populations that "missed" them did not. This is not targeted at individual epistatic interactions but is a population-level statistical argument about how many adaptive paths are locked behind prior mutations. Together, these results establish that the accessible landscape is continuously restructured by evolution itself: earlier gains change the set of available later gains, and the fitness landscape at generation 50,000 is not the same as at generation 0. This connects naturally to the pervasive sign epistasis documented by [[papers/Couce2024_ChangingFitnessEffects|Couce2024]] and to the eco-evolutionary feedbacks that arise from clade coexistence, which add a further ecological layer of epistasis: the selective value of any mutation depends not only on current genetic background but on the frequency of the competing clade.

A theoretical framework developed by [[papers/Maltas2020_TunableFitnessLandscapes|Maltas2020]] treats epistasis as a tunable parameter: the ruggedness $\sigma$ of a "rough Mt. Fuji" landscape parameterizes the amplitude of epistatic interactions. This allows the epistasis level to be varied continuously in simulation, revealing that epistasis has a critical and non-monotonic effect on adaptation in fluctuating environments. Without epistasis (smooth landscapes), alternating environments has minimal impact on evolutionary dynamics. With epistasis (rugged landscapes), the consequences of switching depend on the correlation structure between landscape pairs: positive interlandscape correlation combined with epistasis produces fitness-enhancing dynamics (escape from local optima via shared maxima); anticorrelated landscapes combined with epistasis produce ergodic-like dynamics and reduced average fitness. This framing establishes epistasis not just as a constraint on evolution but as an *amplifier* of environmental correlation effects.

## Key papers

- Bakerlee2022 demonstrated that fitness-correlated trends (FCTs) in a 10-mutation, 1024-genotype *S. cerevisiae* landscape arise from idiosyncratic pairwise interactions rather than non-specific global fitness coupling: FCTs disappear when genotypes are partitioned by 2–3 specific interacting background mutations, and on average just 4 idiosyncratic interactions (out of a median 53 total inferred terms) suffice to recapitulate the full FCT slope within 0.01, capturing 89% of FCT variance [[papers/Bakerlee2022_IdiosyncraticEpistasis|Bakerlee2022]]

- Bakerlee2022 found that fitness landscapes in *S. cerevisiae* are substantially environment-specific (low between-environment correlations of genotype fitnesses and pairwise interactions across 6 environments), that additive and pairwise terms dominate variance with higher-order contributions diminishing rapidly, and that epistatic variance is concentrated in a small number of terms — establishing a sparse, environment-dependent structure consistent with the global epistasis patterns described by Kryazhimskiy2014 and Ardell2024 [[papers/Bakerlee2022_IdiosyncraticEpistasis|Bakerlee2022]]

- Kryazhimskiy2014 established that global diminishing returns epistasis is the dominant form of epistasis in *S. cerevisiae* adaptation: 104 sequenced clones from 13 Founder backgrounds showed that lines from the same Founder share no more mutations at the gene or GO level than lines from different Founders — ruling out modular and idiosyncratic epistasis; targeted knockouts of *GAT2*, *WHI2*, *SFL1* in 18 backgrounds confirmed fitness effects are negatively correlated with background fitness with no background-specific idiosyncratic deviations [[papers/Kryazhimskiy2014_GlobalEpistasis|Kryazhimskiy2014]]

- Kryazhimskiy2014 established the rule of declining adaptability and identified fitness (not genotype) as the sufficient statistic for predicting future adaptation: Founder fitness explains 46% of variance in fitness increment after 500 generations while specific Founder genotype explains only 4%; the rule holds across prokaryotes and eukaryotes in rich and minimal media, suggesting it is a universal consequence of global epistasis [[papers/Kryazhimskiy2014_GlobalEpistasis|Kryazhimskiy2014]]

- Maltas2020 showed that landscape ruggedness (a model of epistasis, parameterized by $\sigma$) is necessary for alternating environments to produce fitness advantages over static evolution — smooth landscapes ($\sigma = 0$) show no benefit from positively correlated switching, while rugged landscapes ($\sigma = 1$) do, with the effect growing as ruggedness increases [[papers/Maltas2020_TunableFitnessLandscapes|Maltas2020]]

- Maltas2020 demonstrated that epistasis interacts with interlandscape correlation to determine the frequency of shared fitness maxima: rugged landscape pairs have a dramatically higher probability of sharing at least one maximum than smooth pairs at the same positive correlation, explaining why ruggedness amplifies the fitness advantage of correlated-environment switching [[papers/Maltas2020_TunableFitnessLandscapes|Maltas2020]]

- Ardell2024 demonstrated that global epistasis slopes $b_m$ are nearly environment-invariant for individual mutations in *S. cerevisiae*: 87% of pairwise slope comparisons across 6 environments are statistically indistinguishable, and an invariant-slope model explains 43% of mutation-effect variance versus 45% for a fully environment-specific model — only 2% difference; 98% of significant slopes are negative (diminishing returns) [[papers/Ardell2024_GlobalEpistasis|Ardell2024]]

- Ardell2024 showed that the environment modulates only one effective parameter of global epistasis — the pivot growth rate $\bar{\lambda}_e$ — and that DFE moments (mean, variance, skewness) collapse onto universal environment-independent curves when expressed as a function of adjusted background fitness $\lambda_{ge}^* = \lambda_{ge} - \bar{\lambda}_e$; DFE mean and skewness cross zero at the pivot, variance is minimized there; predictions validated without free parameters against a 163-strain YPD dataset [[papers/Ardell2024_GlobalEpistasis|Ardell2024]]

- Ardell2024 found that no mutation was unconditionally beneficial or deleterious across 42 *S. cerevisiae* strains and 6 environments: 94% (88/94) of mutations were beneficial in at least one context and 96% of those were also deleterious in at least one, establishing sign reversal as the rule rather than the exception — a consequence of global epistasis that becomes predictable rather than stochastic once the pivot growth rate is known [[papers/Ardell2024_GlobalEpistasis|Ardell2024]]

- Michel2008 demonstrated that drug-level epistasis ($\varepsilon$, the shape of the MIC isobologram) is a primary determinant of the resistance frequency surface $F_{XY}(C_X, C_Y)$: the model $F_{XY} = \iint \eta_{XY}(C_X/x, C_Y/y) p_{XY}(x,y) dx dy$ shows that drugs' interaction geometry ($\eta_{XY}$) directly controls how the population's resistance phenotype distribution ($p_{XY}$) translates into growth in combination environments; synergistic epistasis expands the MSW while antagonism contracts it — establishing drug-level epistasis as a designable parameter for limiting resistance evolution [[papers/Michel2008_MultidrugsResistance|Michel2008]]

- Maltas2019b identified mutations in known antibiotic resistance genes (marR efflux regulator, penicillin-binding protein EF\_1148, ABC transporter EF\_2227) in *E. faecalis* populations adapted to nonantibiotic stressors (osmotic stress, NaBz, biocides), noting that phenotype-genotype relationships are complicated by epistasis and genetic background — the same mutation produces different collateral profiles depending on what other mutations are already present [[papers/Maltas2019b_NonantibioticStressors|Maltas2019b]]

- Couce2024 quantified the pervasiveness of sign epistasis over evolutionary time in *E. coli* LTEE: only 5.9% of beneficial mutations available to the ancestor remain beneficial at 2,000 generations; >90% become neutral or deleterious; the DFE of ancestrally-beneficial mutations in evolved backgrounds regresses completely to the parent distribution mean — establishing that sign epistasis is so pervasive among beneficial mutations that it essentially fully renews the accessible landscape on the timescale of thousands of generations [[papers/Couce2024_ChangingFitnessEffects|Couce2024]]

- Good2017 provided direct statistical evidence for potentiation as a general mechanism: ~16 "missed opportunities" — populations expected but failing to acquire mutations in target genes — occur significantly more than a random permutation null (p~0.003); interpreted as populations lacking prior potentiating mutations that made those adaptive paths accessible in other lineages; establishes that historical contingency operates at scale across 12 independent populations over 60,000 generations [[papers/Good2017_MolecularEvolutionLTEE|Good2017]]

- Good2017 showed that gene mutation appearance times are non-randomly distributed over LTEE evolution — globally over-dispersed relative to chance (p<0.001) — indicating a mixture of coupon-collecting (early depletion of strongly beneficial, abundant targets) and historical contingency (late appearance of potentiation-dependent targets); the balance shifts toward contingency at later timepoints, showing that the epistatic structure of the adaptive landscape itself changes as evolution proceeds [[papers/Good2017_MolecularEvolutionLTEE|Good2017]]

- Neidhart2014 established the mathematical framework for the RMF model as a formalization of tunable epistasis: the ratio $\theta = c/\sqrt{\text{Var}(\eta)}$ controls the balance between a deterministic fitness gradient (epistasis-free, additive pressure toward $\sigma^*$) and random i.i.d. fitness contributions (the epistatic component); for Gumbel-distributed randomness, the expected number of local maxima is $\mathcal{M} \approx 2^L/(L\cosh(c)+1)$, interpolating from the maximally rugged HoC value $2^L/L$ (pure epistasis) to 1 (additive, no epistasis) — providing an explicit formula connecting the epistasis level to landscape ruggedness [[papers/Neidhart2014_RMFModel|Neidhart2014]]

- Neidhart2014 demonstrated that single-step adaptive walk statistics are largely independent of the epistatic ruggedness parameter $c$ (well approximated by the uncorrelated HoC model), while full adaptive walk length depends strongly on $c$ through an additional linear term in initial Hamming distance $d$; this establishes that the evolutionary consequences of epistasis are more visible over longer adaptive trajectories than at the single-step level [[papers/Neidhart2014_RMFModel|Neidhart2014]]

- Couce2024 showed that epistatic changes in the fitness effects of specific mutations — both in the deleterious tail (3-6% of mutations per evolved strain had significantly changed effects over 50K generations) and in gene essentiality (~17% of ancestral essential genes changed status in at least one lineage) — occurred in parallel across independent LTEE lineages more than expected by chance, demonstrating that epistasis generates repeatable evolutionary contingency: the same mutational vulnerabilities and redundancies are repeatedly uncovered by selection acting on the same physiological architecture [[papers/Couce2024_ChangingFitnessEffects|Couce2024]]

## Key concepts and methods

- **Rough Mt. Fuji landscape ruggedness parameter $\theta = c/\sqrt{\text{Var}(\eta)}$** — ratio of additive slope $c$ to standard deviation of random fitness component; controls landscape topology: $\theta \to \infty$ (additive, 1 maximum), $\theta \to 0$ (maximally rugged HoC, $2^L/L$ maxima); introduced by [[papers/Neidhart2014_RMFModel|Neidhart2014]] as a simplification of Aita et al. (2000); applied with Gaussian noise (ruggedness $\sigma = \sqrt{\text{Var}(\eta)}$) in [[papers/Maltas2020_TunableFitnessLandscapes|Maltas2020]]
- **Global epistasis** — the tendency for mutation fitness effects to scale with background genotype fitness (diminishing returns); captured by slope $b_m$ in the generalized global epistasis equation; nearly environment-invariant in *S. cerevisiae* [[papers/Ardell2024_GlobalEpistasis|Ardell2024]]
- **Pivot growth rate $\bar{\lambda}_e$** — the environment-specific background growth rate at which an average mutation switches sign; the single effective parameter through which environment modulates the DFE; characterizes "environmental permissibility" [[papers/Ardell2024_GlobalEpistasis|Ardell2024]]
- **Idiosyncratic epistasis $\xi_{mge}$** — mutation-specific, genotype-specific, environment-specific residual variance not captured by global epistasis; represents the genuinely unpredictable component of mutational effects [[papers/Ardell2024_GlobalEpistasis|Ardell2024]]
- **Fitness-correlated trend (FCT)** — tendency for a mutation's fitness effect to correlate with background fitness; quantified as regression slope $b$ of $\phi_\text{Mut}$ vs. $\phi_\text{WT}$; $b < 1$ indicates diminishing returns; can arise from either global coupling or sparse idiosyncratic interactions; ~44% of mutations in a 10-locus *S. cerevisiae* landscape show significant FCTs [[papers/Bakerlee2022_IdiosyncraticEpistasis|Bakerlee2022]]
- **CRISPR gene drive for landscape construction** — hierarchical Cre-Lox + Cas9 system iterating mate-drive-sporulate-select cycles; each cycle doubles mutations incorporated; constructs complete $2^N$ genotype libraries up to $N = 16$ in 4 cycles; enables genome-spanning combinatorial landscapes paired with DNA barcode bulk fitness assays [[papers/Bakerlee2022_IdiosyncraticEpistasis|Bakerlee2022]]
- **Sign epistasis** — fitness effect of a mutation changes sign depending on genetic background; mechanistic basis for local fitness peaks in empirical landscapes
- **Whole-genome sequencing (breseq pipeline)** — used to identify candidate resistance mutations in evolved *E. faecalis* populations; epistatic interactions between identified mutations not fully resolved [[papers/Maltas2019b_NonantibioticStressors|Maltas2019b]]

## Open questions

- How common is sign epistasis among antibiotic resistance mutations in *E. faecalis*, and does it vary by drug class?
- Does global epistasis slope invariance hold under mechanistically targeted perturbations like antibiotic drug exposure — or does drug-specific selection reshape the slope distribution? [[papers/Ardell2024_GlobalEpistasis|Ardell2024]] explicitly flags this as open
- Can the pivot growth rate framework be applied to *E. faecalis* resistance evolution — does the DFE for new mutations shift predictably as populations adapt to drug (background fitness increases relative to the pivot), implying fewer available beneficial mutations and slower late-stage adaptation?
- Does epistasis between resistance mutations create predictable constraints on evolutionary trajectories, or does epistasis primarily add stochasticity?
- How does the genetic background (strain, species) affect the magnitude and sign of epistatic interactions? Maltas2019b notes that the same mutations can produce different collateral phenotypes across backgrounds [[papers/Maltas2019b_NonantibioticStressors|Maltas2019b]]
- Can the ruggedness level $\sigma$ of the Maltas2020 model be calibrated to empirical resistance landscape data? [[papers/Maltas2020_TunableFitnessLandscapes|Maltas2020]]
- When epistasis is strong enough to create shared maxima even between anticorrelated (mutual collateral sensitivity) landscape pairs, does this undermine MDP-based drug cycling strategies? How large is the effect under realistic population sizes and switching timescales?
- Does the near-complete renewal of the accessible beneficial landscape (via sign epistasis) over ~2,000 generations of bacterial evolution occur at similar timescales in antibiotic resistance contexts — or does the mechanistic constraint of resistance pathways (efflux, target alteration) slow the turnover compared to general metabolic adaptation in the LTEE? [[papers/Couce2024_ChangingFitnessEffects|Couce2024]]
- Does the ~4-interaction explanation for FCTs generalize to antibiotic resistance landscapes, where mutations are concentrated in a few functional modules (efflux, target alteration, permeability)? Pathway-concentrated mutations might produce FCTs requiring more terms to decompose — or, conversely, more easily partitioned FCTs if the relevant interacting mutations are known [[papers/Bakerlee2022_IdiosyncraticEpistasis|Bakerlee2022]]
- What specific potentiating mutations underlie the ~16 missed opportunities identified by Good2017? Can they be identified from existing metagenomic sequence data by looking for mutations shared in populations that acquired the contingent gene but absent in those that did not — and would this reveal predictable epistatic modules? [[papers/Good2017_MolecularEvolutionLTEE|Good2017]]
- Does the coupon-collecting → historical contingency shift observed in LTEE gene appearance times apply to antibiotic resistance evolution, where mechanistic constraint may compress or eliminate the coupon-collecting phase and push the system directly into the contingency-dominated regime? [[papers/Good2017_MolecularEvolutionLTEE|Good2017]]
