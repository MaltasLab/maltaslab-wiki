## What this wiki is

A persistent, structured knowledge base for a research lab, maintained entirely by you.
The PI and students curate sources and ask questions. You do the summarizing, cross-referencing,
filing, and bookkeeping. The wiki compounds — every paper you ingest makes the next one richer.

---

## Field and systems

The lab uses tools from statistical physics, population genetics, evolutionary game thoery, and computational biology to understand how microscopic growth processes and genome dynamics at the single cell level give rise to the collective behaviors that can be observed at the population level. Our work ranges from experimental evolution experiments using Enterrococus faecium, Saccharomyces cerevisiae, or non-small cell lung cancer, to purely theoretical population genetic studies such as fixation time under frequency-dependent selection. The lab has recently focused on frequency-dependent interactions between sub-populations, evolution of collateral sensitivity, and predicting evolution in multi-drug environments. Through these specific examples we seek to uncover unifying theoretical principles that could help us understand, forecast, and ultimately control the evolutionary dynamics that take place in these diverse scenarios.

### Topic scope definitions

These definitions are strict. When a paper could plausibly fit multiple topics, use
them to assign correctly rather than tagging broadly.

**`evolution-in-multidrug-environments`** — populations evolving under **simultaneous**
exposure to two or more drugs at the same time. The selective pressure is a combination
of drugs applied together in the same environment. Do NOT use this topic for sequential
drug treatment (alternating or cycling drugs one at a time).

**`evolution-in-fluctuating-environments`** — populations evolving under **sequential**
or **alternating** environment (such as drug, or simply season change) exposure, 
where the environment changes over time. This includes drug cycling, alternating therapies, 
and any protocol where drugs are applied one at a time in sequence. Do NOT use this topic 
for simultaneous drug combinations.

**`evolutionary-control`** — papers focused on the design and optimization of treatment
strategies to steer or constrain evolutionary trajectories. This includes optimal drug
scheduling, MDP/control-theoretic frameworks, evolutionary steering, and adaptive therapy.
Papers about collateral sensitivity *as a tool for control* belong here as well as in
`collateral-sensitivity`. Papers that simply observe collateral effects without framing
them as a control strategy belong only in `collateral-sensitivity`.

**`collateral-sensitivity`** — the phenomenon of evolved resistance to one drug being
associated with increased sensitivity to another. Papers here characterize, measure, or
model collateral effects. Papers that also design control strategies based on those
effects should additionally be tagged `evolutionary-control`.

These four topics frequently co-occur. A paper can and should be tagged with multiple
topics when it genuinely contributes to each. The definitions above tell you *which*
contribution goes to *which* hub — not just whether the paper gets tagged.

## File naming conventions

- All filenames: lowercase, hyphens for spaces, no special characters
- Paper files: `AuthorYear_ShortTitle.md` (e.g., `Kim2023_CollateralSensitivity.md`)
- Topic directories: lowercase, hyphenated (e.g., `drug-combinations-antibiotics/`)
- Wikilinks: always use relative `[[PageName]]` syntax
- Never use spaces or special characters in any filename

**Same first-author, same year — disambiguation:**
Before creating any paper file, check `_index.md` and `references.bib` for existing
entries with the same `AuthorYear` cite key. If a conflict exists, append a letter suffix
to all three artifacts — the filename, the wikilink, and the BibTeX cite key — keeping
them in sync:

| Conflict | Filename | Cite key | Wikilink display |
|---|---|---|---|
| First paper | `Kim2023_TitleA.md` | `Kim2023a` | `Kim2023a` |
| Second paper | `Kim2023_TitleB.md` | `Kim2023b` | `Kim2023b` |

Apply suffixes retroactively if a conflict is only discovered on the second ingest —
rename the existing file, update its BibTeX key, and find and replace all wikilinks
to it across the wiki before proceeding. Report to PI when this happens.

## How ideas propagate from papers

This is the most important rule in the schema. Read it carefully.

A paper page (`wiki/papers/`) is a holistic summary of the whole paper.
Propagation is different: specific claims, findings, and methods from the paper are
placed in their relevant pages across the wiki, each with a specific citation.

**The same paper will be cited multiple times across the wiki. This is correct.**
Each citation describes what that paper contributes *to that specific page* —
not just "this paper is relevant here."

### Two kinds of contributions — both must be propagated

**1. Specific findings** — empirical results, measurements, model outputs, observations.
These go in the Key papers section of topic hubs with specific cited sentences.

**2. Conceptual advances** — how the paper changes the framing of the problem at a
population or systems level. What it establishes about the structure of the system,
not just what it measured. What new strategies, frameworks, or theoretical principles
it introduces. These go into the "What this topic is about" section of topic hubs,
updating the prose to reflect the evolved understanding.

This lab's work focuses on extracting population-level and systems-level understanding,
not cataloging specific mechanisms. For every paper, ask: **what does this paper change
about how we think about the problem?** That answer must be captured — not just the
experimental results.

For example, a paper that shows collateral sensitivity profiles are heterogeneous but
statistically structured by drug class does two things: (1) it reports specific measurements
(specific finding → Key papers), and (2) it shifts the conceptual frame from "collateral
effects are unpredictable" to "collateral effects are stochastic but tractably structured
at the population level" (conceptual advance → update "What this topic is about"). Both
must be captured. The second is often more important for this lab.

### Updating "What this topic is about"

This section should read like a coherent argument about what we currently understand
and what remains open — not a list of results. After ingesting a paper with a significant
conceptual contribution, rewrite or extend this section to reflect the updated understanding.
Name the conceptual advance explicitly. If a paper introduces a framework or principle
that the field will build on, that framework should be described in this section with
a citation, not buried in a results list.

When updating any page during ingest:
- Write a specific sentence describing what this paper contributes to this page
- Include the citation as a wikilink: `[[papers/AuthorYear_ShortTitle|AuthorYear]]`
- Never just append a paper title to a list without a specific contribution sentence
- Always ask: does this paper also advance the conceptual framing? If so, update
  "What this topic is about" in addition to Key papers

Example — the Maltas & Wood 2019 PLoS Biology paper on collateral sensitivity in
*E. faecalis*, which both characterizes collateral effects extensively and introduces
an MDP framework for optimal drug scheduling:

- `topics/collateral-sensitivity/_hub.md` → Key papers receives the specific finding:
  "Maltas2019 showed that collateral effects are pervasive across 900 mutant-drug
  combinations in *E. faecalis* but highly heterogeneous, with profiles clustering
  by drug class rather than individual drugs
  [[papers/Maltas2019_CollateralSensitivity|Maltas2019]]"

- `topics/collateral-sensitivity/_hub.md` → "What this topic is about" updated to
  reflect the conceptual advance: that apparent unpredictability at the level of
  individual mutants coexists with tractable statistical structure at the population
  level — and that this structure is sufficient to enable rational control strategies

- `topics/evolutionary-control/_hub.md` → Key papers receives the MDP contribution:
  "Maltas2019 introduced a Markov decision process framework for designing optimal
  drug policies that outperform intuitive cycling protocols by steering populations
  toward vulnerable future states rather than maximizing short-term efficacy alone
  [[papers/Maltas2019_CollateralSensitivity|Maltas2019]]"

- `topics/evolutionary-control/_hub.md` → "What this topic is about" updated to
  introduce the concept of evolutionary steering: that optimal control may require
  occasional use of less effective drugs to guide the population toward a more
  vulnerable future state — a counterintuitive but experimentally validated principle

- `topics/evolution-in-fluctuating-environments/_hub.md` → Key papers receives the
  sequential drug scheduling finding: "Maltas2019 demonstrated experimentally that
  MDP-derived drug sequences reduce cumulative growth and adaptation rates relative
  to naive cycling protocols across single-drug, two-drug, and four-drug comparisons
  [[papers/Maltas2019_CollateralSensitivity|Maltas2019]]"

- `concepts/markov-decision-process.md` → created or updated with the MDP framework
  as applied to evolutionary control problems

Five citations across four pages, capturing both specific results and conceptual advances.
The topic hubs are richer in argument, not just in citations.

## Paper page format

Every paper page must begin with this YAML frontmatter:

```yaml
---
title: ""
authors: []           # Last name, First initial — e.g. [Smith J, Kim A]
year:
venue: ""             # Journal or conference name
doi: ""               # Include if available
arxiv: ""             # Include if available
topics: []            # One or more topic slugs, or "pending"
lab_paper: false      # true if PI is a co-author
canonical: false      # true if ingested as a bootstrapping/foundational paper
relevance: high       # high / medium / low
ingested_by: ""       # name of person who ran the ingest
---
```

Followed by these sections in order:
```markdown
## Summary
[3–5 sentence plain-language summary of what the paper does and finds]

## Conceptual contribution
[What does this paper establish at the population, systems, or theoretical level?
 What does it change about how we think about the problem — not just what it found,
 but what it means? Phrase this as the argument the paper makes, not a list of results.
 For this lab's work especially, this is often the most important section.
 If the paper introduces a framework, principle, or conceptual shift that will be
 built upon, name it explicitly here.]

## Key findings
[Bulleted list of specific findings, phrased as claims with enough detail to be useful
 without reading the paper. Each finding should be self-contained.]

## Methods
[What experimental or computational methods were used. Enough detail to understand
 what was actually done.]

## Relationship to existing wiki
[How does this paper connect to, extend, or contradict what's already in the wiki?
 Explicit links to relevant concept, method, and topic pages.]

## Open questions raised
[Questions this paper surfaces but does not answer. Will be propagated to topic hubs.]

## Conversion notes
[Only if conversion was imperfect. Describe what was missing or garbled.]
```

## Topic hub format (`_hub.md`)

```markdown
# [Topic Name]

## What this topic is about
[2–3 paragraph plain-language description. Updated as understanding matures.
 Written to be readable by a new rotation student with no background in this area.]

## Key papers
[Each entry: one sentence describing the specific contribution of this paper to this topic,
 followed by the citation. Not just a list of titles.]

## Key concepts and methods
[Wikilinks to relevant concept and method pages, each with a one-line annotation.]

## Open questions
[Unanswered questions surfaced by the literature. Each question notes which papers
 raised or bear on it. Updated with every relevant ingest.]

## Review article outline
[A living section outline for a potential review on this topic.
 Each section notes: coverage strength (strong/developing/thin) and gaps to fill.]

## Cross-topic connections
[Links to other topic hubs where this area overlaps, with a one-sentence explanation
 of the connection.]
```

## Ingest workflow (follow in order, every time)

1. **Convert** — run `./convert.sh` on the PDF; confirm `.md` was produced
2. **Read** — read the converted `.md` in full before doing anything else
3. **Discuss** — briefly surface key claims, what's new relative to the wiki,
   which topics this touches. Wait for PI input if the session is interactive.
4. **Add BibTeX entry** — append a complete entry to `references.bib` with cite key
   matching `AuthorYear` format
5. **Write paper page** — create `wiki/papers/AuthorYear_ShortTitle.md` with
   full frontmatter and all required sections
6. **Update `_index.md`** — add the paper with a one-line summary and topic tags
7. **Append to `_log.md`** — format: `## [YYYY-MM-DD] ingest | AuthorYear_Title`
   followed by 2–3 sentences on what was updated
8. **Propagate ideas** — update concept and method pages with specific cited contributions;
   use `[[papers/AuthorYear_ShortTitle|AuthorYear]]` wikilink format for all citations
9. **Update topic hubs** — for each tagged topic, add specific cited contributions to
   Key papers, update Open questions, update Review article outline coverage notes;
   use wikilink citations throughout
10. **Update `lab/overview.md`** — if this is a bootstrapping paper (lab_paper or canonical),
    update the factual lab description
11. **Handle pending** — if topics = pending, append signal to `_emerging_topics.md`
12. **Commit** — `git add -A && git commit -m "ingest: AuthorYear_Title — updates [list pages touched]"`

Do not skip steps. Do not combine steps 5–9 into a single pass — read the paper first,
then update each page deliberately.

## Math

Use KaTeX syntax for all mathematical expressions. Do not approximate equations with
plain text — write them properly so they render on the site.

- Inline math: `$...$` — e.g. `$\Delta G = -RT \ln K$`
- Display math: `$$...$$` on its own line — e.g. `$$\frac{dN}{dt} = rN\left(1-\frac{N}{K}\right)$$`

KaTeX is enabled in `quartz.config.ts`. No additional tooling required.

## Citations

**Inline citations in wiki pages use wikilinks, not formatted text.**

When citing a paper on a concept, method, topic, or any other wiki page, use:
```
[[papers/AuthorYear_ShortTitle|AuthorYear]]
```
This renders as a clickable link (e.g. "Kim2023") that navigates directly to the paper's
full summary page. Do not write static citation strings like "(Kim et al., 2023)".
Do not use @BibTeX syntax in wiki pages.

**`references.bib` is maintained as a parallel artifact for manuscript use.**

During every ingest, add a BibTeX entry to `references.bib` in the repo root.
The cite key must always match the `AuthorYear` format used in filenames and wikilinks.

```bibtex
@article{Kim2023,
  author  = {Kim, S. and Lee, A.},
  title   = {Full paper title here},
  journal = {Journal Name},
  year    = {2023},
  volume  = {},
  pages   = {},
  doi     = {}
}
```

Use `@article` for journal papers, `@inproceedings` for conference papers,
`@preprint` or `@misc` with a note for arXiv preprints.
Fill in every field you can extract from the paper. Leave fields blank rather than guessing.

1. **Never modify any file in `raw/`** — these are immutable source documents
2. **Never delete wiki pages** — mark as deprecated with a note at the top if needed
3. **Never invent citations** — if uncertain about a claim, flag with `[VERIFY]`

6. **Always write specific contribution sentences** when updating pages — never just list papers
7. **Always read the full paper before updating any wiki pages**
8. **File substantive query answers back into the wiki** as new pages
