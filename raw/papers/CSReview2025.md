# Collateral sensitivity and genetic vulnerability of antibiotic resistance

Kevin Yang<sup>1</sup> , Aviram Rasouly 1,2, \*, and Evgeny Nudler 1,2, \*

Antibiotic combination therapy has a critical role in limiting emergent antibiotic resistance in bacterial pathogens. Collateral sensitivity (CS), resistance to one antibiotic that is inextricable from sensitivity to another antibiotic, presents an opportunity for combinations explicitly selecting against resistance. Complementing efforts to select against resistance, differential genetic vulnerability mapping unravels dependencies unique for resistant strains. In this review, we discuss the potential implications of CS and genetic vulnerability data for the design of bespoke antibiotic combinations, drug discovery, and tracking evolution of resistant strains.

# Barriers and opportunities for the implementation of collateral sensitivity and genetic vulnerability principles in antibiotic therapy

Antibiotic resistance often comes with a cost to bacterial fitness. Resistance mutations and genes adversely affect growth rate, nutritional requirements, and tolerance to lethal stress. By itself, this competitive disadvantage in an antibiotic-free environment is difficult to leverage against resistant strains in clinical settings. In part, this is because the fitness cost of antibiotic resistance (see [Glossary\)](#page-1-0), even in its pristine state, is usually not high enough to deplete resistance from bacterial populations [\[1](#page-10-0)]. Besides, continuous evolution of resistant strains selects for cost compensatory mutations with a potential to stabilize resistance in bacterial populations.

Instead, efforts to keep resistance at bay focus on the design of antibiotics in combinations, either with each other or with adjuvants [[2\]](#page-10-0). Combination of multiple antibiotics suppresses resistance because multi-drug resistance takes longer to evolve. Other benefits of combination therapy are increased efficacy and reduced toxicity. A combination of an antibiotic with an adjuvant – a small molecule with no standalone antimicrobial attributes – is a more deliberate strategy aiming to negate resistance by disabling the mechanism of resistance. However, its clinical utility is proven primarily for lactam antibiotics [[3\]](#page-10-0).

Over the past decade, research on collateral sensitivity (CS) and cross resistance (XR) has tested ways to make use of the cost inherent in resistance to guide design of antibiotic combinations and regimens [\[4](#page-10-0)–7]. A mutation or a gene conferring resistance to one antibiotic may either make bacteria more sensitive (CS), or resistant (XR) to another antibiotic [\(Figure](#page-2-0) 1). Studies demonstrated how cycling between said antibiotics is superior to other combinations in protracting emergent antibiotic resistance under laboratory conditions [8–[10\]](#page-10-0).

In this review we discuss barriers ([Figure](#page-2-0) 2) for implementation of CS principles to improve antibiotic therapy outcomes, as well as opportunities that go beyond design of antibiotic combinations. A major barrier is the variation in CS and XR profiles across strains resistant to the same antibiotic ([Figure](#page-2-0) 2A,B) [\[7](#page-10-0),[11\]](#page-10-0). # Importance of high coverage of resistance mutations and genes in CS studies

Early CS studies used two experimental approaches that do not lend themselves to high coverage of resistance mutations and genes. The first consists of profiling susceptibility of resistant bacteria, either laboratory strains or clinical isolates, to antimicrobials from different classes. A second approach entails adaptive evolution of antibiotic resistance and testing for any concomitant CS in clones isolated from evolved populations. Both approaches are predisposed for CS discovery in resistant strains that are frequently isolated in the laboratory or are common among clinical isolates. The high prevalence of such strains may emanate from higher mutation rates at some sites in the genome [\[14\]](#page-10-0), the fitness of the resistant bacteria [[15\]](#page-10-0), or the level of resistance conferred [\[16\]](#page-10-0). As such, CS profiles of low-frequency resistant mutants, low-level resistance mutants, and other groups of resistant mutants are missing.

To showcase the variation in antibiotic susceptibility profile within a group of mutants resistant to the same antibiotic, let us consider resistance to rifampicin (RIF). RIF binds bacterial RNA polymerase (RNAP) and inhibits transcription past the stage of initiation [\[17\]](#page-10-0). At the level of amino-acid sequence, the RIF binding site is highly conserved and surprisingly mutable [[18\]](#page-10-0). Nearly all mutations are located across four regions of the β subunit, the so-called RIF resistance determining regions (RRDRs). The mechanism of resistance is the same in all cases, blocking RIF from binding RNAP [\[19](#page-10-0)]. Where the mutations differ from one another is in the effect they have on RNAP at each step of the transcription cycle, its response to signaling molecules, and interaction with transcription factors. Consequently, RIF resistant mutants present a wide range of diverging phenotypes.

As might be expected, the phenotypic divergence extends to a high degree of variation in CS and XR profiles among RIF resistant mutants. In a recent chemical genetics campaign in Escherichia coli, 32 RIF-resistant mutants were profiled for CS and XR against 82 antimicrobials and other stressors [\[20](#page-10-0)]. The study demonstrated high variation in CS and XR profile across RIF-resistant mutants but also points to clusters of mutants with a similar susceptibility profile.

In the era of sequencing data, high variation in CS and XR profiles can also be advantageous. It implies that the probability of discovering strong CS of a given resistance mutation or gene is also higher. A strong CS supports specific targeting of resistant strains while applying a weaker natural selection of resistance on antibiotic-sensitive strains. Genomic data of resistant strains is increasingly available from patients with chronic infections [\[21\]](#page-10-0), or as part of pathogen surveillance efforts within hospital systems [[22\]](#page-10-0). In the future, real-time sequencing of pathogens in clinical samples taken from patients may benefit from CS and XR data too.

# Glossary

Collateral sensitivity (CS): resistance mutations and genes conferring resistance to one antibiotic render bacteria more sensitive to another antibiotic.

Cost compensatory mutations: the continuous evolution by natural selection of resistant strains is driven by fitnessimproving mutations. Such mutations prevent depletion of resistant bacterial cells from a mixed population of resistant and sensitive bacterial cells in an antibiotic-free environment. They also erode the CS of resistant strains. Costmitigating and cost-compounding genes are hotspots for compensatory mutations.

Cost compounding genes: genes with an activity that exacerbates the fitness cost of antibiotic resistance.

Cost mitigating genes: genes with an activity that limits the fitness cost of antibiotic resistance.

Cross resistance (XR): resistance mutations and genes conferring resistance to one antibiotic render bacteria resistant to a second antibiotic as well.

#### Differential genetic vulnerability:

genetic interactions between resistance mutations and genes to the rest of the bacterial genome with a unique effect on the fitness of resistant strains.

Fitness cost of antibiotic resistance: the adverse effect resistance mutations and genes impose on the fitness of bacteria. The extent of cost varies considerably between resistance mutations and genes. The most apparent manifestation of fitness cost is a lower growth rate of some resistant strains. Fitness cost can manifest in other forms such as a lower level of tolerance or resistance to stress.

Genetic epistasis over resistance: resistance mutations and genes are necessary but not sufficient for the expression of the resistance phenotype. Genes facilitating the expression of the resistance phenotype are said to be epistatic over resistance.

Synthetic lethality: two mutations, each compatible with bacterial growth on its own, together inhibit bacterial growth. In the context of the genetic vulnerability of antibiotic resistance strains, a resistance mutation or gene may present synthetic lethality with inactivating mutations in cost-mitigating gene(s) or activating mutations in costcompounding genes.

What is the mechanistic basis for such strong CS? The antimicrobial activity of 5FU is dependent on pyrimidine salvage pathways for prodrug activation, inhibition of thymidylate synthase (ThyA), and consequently depletion of thymidine triphosphate (dTTP) pools [\[25](#page-10-0)]. With no dTTP available during DNA replication, bacteria die of a so-called thymineless death (TLD). In this RIF-resistant cluster, a higher rate of transcription elongation puts a strain on the pyrimidine ribonucleotide pool [\[18](#page-10-0)]. Because dTTP synthesis branches out from the pyrimidine ribonucleotide biosynthesis pathway, the ripple effect of 'fast' RNAP festers to limit not only UTP but also dTTP pools. The already strained dTTP pools of 'fast' RNAP mutants sensitize them to TLD at lower concentrations of 5FU [\[18\]](#page-10-0).

# Discovery of collateral sensitivities within the same class of antibiotics

Prominent examples of CS with a known mechanism showcase mutants resistant to one class of antibiotics which are more sensitive to antibiotics from an unrelated class [\[5](#page-10-0),[26](#page-10-0)].

Until recently, there was a dearth of examples of mutations or genes conferring resistance to one antibiotic that sensitizes strains to a second antibiotic within the same class. Perhaps a corollary of a study design prioritizing inclusion of different classes of antimicrobials due to a limitation on the total number of antimicrobials tested. Or a consequence of a high proportion of CS studies where resistance is driven by mutations outside of antibiotic-binding sites.

Recent studies provide ample evidence for within-class CS, especially in the case of mutations residing at the antibiotic-binding site of either the antibiotic target or an inactivating enzyme. This is noteworthy for a number of reasons. First, in the absence of a robust antibiotic discovery pipeline, derivatization of existing antibiotics is a major source of new antibiotics [[27](#page-10-0)]. Indeed, class size for some antibiotics – for example, the lactams – is impressively high [[28\]](#page-10-0). Second, within-class CS is likely to be conserved across species of bacterial pathogens. This is because it depends solely on the sequence of the antibiotic-binding site. Third, resistance mechanisms to antibiotics of different classes are generally compatible and yield a strain resistant to both. However, mutually exclusive substrate specificity of antibiotic-binding-site mutants towards within-class antibiotics may limit emergence of mutants resistant to both.

Consider the example of cefotaximase-Munich (CTX-M-15), a common variant of the extended spectrum β-lactamase (ESBL). It confers resistance to penicillins and cephalosporins, but not to mecillinam (MEC), meropenem (MER), or a combination of a penicillin with an ESBL inhibitor such as piperacillin–tazobactam. To gauge the risk of gain-of-function mutations in blaCTX-M-15, a recent study constructed a mutant pool covering 42% of all possible single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) in E. coli [\[29](#page-10-0)]. CTX-M-15 mutants with single amino-acid substitutions confer resistance, beyond the clinical breaking point, to MEC (N135D) and piperacillin–tazobactam (S133G or G239S). For example, mutant N135D increased the MIC to MEC by 50-fold. However, at the expense of MEC inactivation, the mutant enzyme can no longer confer resistance to the cephalosporin cefotaxime. According to a structure of CTX-M-15 in complex with cefotaxime, all three residues are positioned in the vicinity of the active site of the enzyme. As expected, the combination of MEC and cefotaxime inhibits the growth of a mixed population of wild-type (WT) and mutant CTX-M-15.

By the same token, MAGE-seq supported mapping of residues critical for the substrate specificity of the β-lactamase blaampC in E. coli [[30](#page-10-0)]. Tested against piperacillin, aztreonam, or cefepime, no mutant in the pool conferred high-level resistance to all three. Moreover, mutants with increased resistance to both piperacillin and cefepime are more sensitive to aztreonam. And a subset of mutants with increased resistance to aztreonam are more sensitive to piperacillin and cefepime.

The same study highlights another advantage of deep-mutagenesis campaigns. Namely, discovery of rare mutants with a trade-off escaping phenotype. Typically, affinity increasing mutations at the binding site of β-lactamase to the antibiotic confer higher level of resistance, but they also sensitize β-lactamase to inhibitors such as avibactam. However, MAGE-seq reveals mutants, such as Y150A and N346W, resistant to a combination of aztreonam and avibactam [\[30\]](#page-10-0). Fortunately, other combinations of β-lactam and inhibitor are active against all mutants tested. In this way, comprehensive analysis of a binding site mutant may inform of antibiotic-adjuvant combinations impervious to evolution of resistance.

![](_page_5_Picture_1.jpeg)

Mutually exclusive substrate specificity is not limited to ARG mutants. In Mycobacterium tuberculosis, a massive engineering effort produced a library of degron-tagged essential proteins as a means to sensitize a specific essential target to its prospective inhibitor. Screening the library against 50 000 compounds yielded BRD-8000, an inhibitor of the essential efflux pump EfpA [\[31](#page-11-0)]. Discovery of BRD-8000 enabled discovery of a second EfpA inhibitor, BRD-9327, based on a similar chemogenomic signature in the primary screen [\[32\]](#page-11-0). efpA mutants resistant to BRD-8000.3 (an optimized derivate of BRD-8000 active against WT M. tuberculosis) are more sensitive to BRD-9327, and some of the BRD-9327-resistant efpA mutant alleles are more sensitive to BRD-8000.3.

In addition, deep-mutagenesis of antibiotic-binding sites proved instrumental in recent studies on the species-specificity and the bactericidal mechanism of antibiotics. A single amino acid residue in RNAP accounts for the narrow-spectrum activity of the transcription inhibitor fidaxomicin against Clostridium difficile [[33\]](#page-11-0). In E. coli, single amino acid substitutions at the RIF binding site are enough to transform its antimicrobial effect from bacteriostatic to bactericidal [\[18](#page-10-0)].

# High-throughput discovery of CS and XR with chemogenomic data

Discovery of CS and XR in resistant clones isolated from evolution or mutagenesis campaigns share a common feature. For the most part, CS and XR are attributed to a single mutation or gene. This means that the sensitivity of detection of CS and XR is strongly affected by its frequency and resistance level. What if they are too low to capture under a given set of conditions? Also, the molecular basis of CS is not always clear from a single resistance mutation or gene.

To address this gap in knowledge, studies are now taking a more systematic approach for discovery of novel CS and XR interactions. A recent study [[34](#page-11-0)] leverages published data [\[35](#page-11-0)] from the susceptibility profiling of the E. coli gene knock-out collection (Keio collection) to 40 distinct antibiotics. The study applied a new computational model for discovery, focusing on gene knock-outs (KOs) with a large effect on fitness in response to each mate in a pair of antibiotics. Because the model-based discovery is derived from fitness scores of many KO mutants, it has a remarkable sensitivity to predict novel interactions [\[34\]](#page-11-0). A group of genes, sharing discordant or concordant susceptibility to a pair of antibiotics, is also a good starting point for understanding the molecular basis of CS.

For example, the study maps two groups of genes, each accounting for a distinct TET resistance mechanism. One group imparts CS, and the other confers XR to azithromycin (AZI). CS interaction of TET-AZI is driven by lipopolysaccharide (LPS) biosynthesis genes. LPS prevents entry of AZI across the outer membrane (OM) but supports entry of TET through OM porin OmpF. Mutants with truncated LPS are therefore TET resistant and AZI sensitive. However, the CS to AZI is short-lived in evolution experiments, and ultimately clones which are neutral or XR to AZI take over. XR interaction between TET-AZI XR is driven by mutations inducing the activity of efflux pumps. The fitness cost of TET resistance is likely lower in this group of mutants.

# CS to non-antibiotics

In many instances, antibiotic-resistant strains present little if any CS. One path forward for CS research on this subset of resistance mutations and genes entails screening of antimicrobials that are not currently in clinical use. Although by definition there is no immediate clinical utility for this approach, there are good reasons to pursue it. The first is that resistant strains can be viewed as a tool for discovery of antimicrobials without strong activity against antibiotic sensitive strains. Subsequent chemical derivatization of lead compounds might produce derivatives with increased antimicrobial activity. Then there is the possibility that, of itself, the lower MIC of a

A recent campaign of CS profiling tested thousands of natural product extracts from soil-dwelling Actinobacteria and Burkholderia against a panel of resistant isogenic E. coli strains [[36\]](#page-11-0). The panel included ceftazidime (CEF)-resistant strains with mutations in LPS biosynthesis genes rfaG or rfaH. It turns out that growth of rfa mutants is extremely sensitive to the natural products borrelidin A and P. Borrelidin A is a known inhibitor of the threonine tRNA ligase ThrS. The CS to borrelidins is not driven by differential accumulation of the compounds in rfa mutants, thus deferring for a future study to unravel the molecular basis of CS [\[36\]](#page-11-0).

For future CS screening, US Food and Drug Administration (FDA)-approved compounds for indications other than infectious diseases [[37](#page-11-0)] are a promising cache of prospective antimicrobials. Also, genetically encoded antimicrobials present an opportunity to evolve antimicrobials against emergent resistance [\[38](#page-11-0)–41].

# Genetic vulnerabilities of antibiotic resistance

The fitness cost of antibiotic resistance is shaped by the interaction of resistance mutations and genes with the rest of the genome [\(Figure](#page-3-0) 3 and [Figure](#page-3-0) 4A,B). In antibiotic-resistant strains, the activity of some genes is expected to mitigate fitness cost. Mutations compromising the activity of said genes are, in extreme cases, synthetic lethal with resistance mutations or genes [[42](#page-11-0),[43\]](#page-11-0). Cost mitigating genes may also minimize CS of antibiotic-resistant strains to other antibiotics ([Figure](#page-3-0) 4C). Conversely, the activity of other genes compounds fitness cost, making it worse than what it would have been without them. Both cost mitigating and cost compounding genes are likely hotspots for the evolution of cost compensatory mutations. Therefore, genome-wide mapping of differential genetic vulnerabilities has implications for selective targeting of resistant strains (Table 1) and for tracking compensatory mutations in clinical isolates.

Differential genetic vulnerability analysis indicated that upp of the uracil salvage pathway is compounding cost in H526Y [\[45](#page-11-0)]. Uracil phosphoribosyl transferase (Upp) generates uridine monophosphate (UMP) from imported uracil. UMP is a precursor for synthesis of both UTP and cytidine triphosphate (CTP), and salvage deficient upp mutant has lower levels of both [[18](#page-10-0)]. Ribonucleotide availability is a limiting factor of transcription elongation in vitro, making it possible that activity of Upp is fueling 'fast' RNAP with UTP and CTP, thereby compounding fitness cost in living cells. In congruence with this model, nascent elongating transcript sequencing (NET-seq) [\[48\]](#page-11-0) indicates that pause frequency of H526Y RNAP is increasing in a background of upp [\[45\]](#page-11-0).

Within the group of cost mitigating genes, there is an enrichment of genes with a function in cell-wall biosynthesis and division. Two top hits are peptidoglycan endopeptidase MepM, and cell-division factor ZapA. Genetic analysis demonstrates synthetic lethal interaction between H526Y and a double KO yebAzapA, as well as CS of H526Y to cell-wall biosynthesis inhibitors, for example, MEC and A22.

The CS to A22 and 5FU is strong enough to select against H526Y and the rest of RIF mutants with a similar CS profile [[45](#page-11-0)].

# From mapping of genetic vulnerabilities to tracking compensatory mutations in clinical isolates of bacterial pathogens

RIF is part of the first-line antibiotic combination to treat patients afflicted with M. tuberculosis [[50](#page-11-0)]. Driving RIF resistance in the majority of clinical isolates is rpoB S450L (S531 in E. coli RNAP). A recent study used clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR) interference (CRISPRi) to map differential genetic vulnerabilities of this mutant in M. tuberculosis [[51](#page-11-0)].

The S450L mutant presents differential vulnerability to depletion of essential transcription elongation factor NusG, as well as to many genes with a function in the translation process [[51\]](#page-11-0). Previous studies established that NusG promotes transcription–translation coupling (TTC) by bridging physical contacts between RNAP and the ribosome [\[52,53\]](#page-11-0); it is also known that S450L RNAP is prone for pausing and transcription termination in vitro [\[54\]](#page-11-0). The authors suggested that efficient TTC may increase the transcription elongation rate and prevent premature termination in S450L.

Curiously, through a separate set of contacts with RNAP, NusG promotes RNAP pausing and transcription termination [\[55](#page-11-0)]. The authors followed-up on the results of the screen with a genome wide association study (GWAS) based on thousands of clinical isolates of M. tuberculosis [[56,57\]](#page-11-0). In RIF-resistant isolates with the S450L mutation, nusG is under diversifying selection according to nonsynonymous to the synonymous (dN/dS) mutation ratio [\[51](#page-11-0)]. Per structural predictions and in vitro transcription termination assays, the mutations accumulate in residues important to the pro-pausing activity of NusG, and compromise it [\[51](#page-11-0)]. The same mutations indeed improve the fitness of RIF resistant S450L in growth competition experiments.

![](_page_8_Picture_1.jpeg)

The study showcases an important value of genetic vulnerability mapping, namely the clinical annotation of secondary, fitness-cost-compensatory, mutations in antibiotic-resistant strains. For example, a secondary mutation may suggest that a resistant isolate is more likely to be transmitted. Or, the CS of a resistant strain to an unrelated antibiotic may erode in case it has a secondary mutation. At the same time, most compensatory mutations from this study did not restore fitness of S450L in full, suggesting that CS may not erode completely.

# Mapping of genetic vulnerabilities unravels the molecular basis of CS

Like RIF, isoniazid (INH) is one of the first-line antibiotics for combination treatment of M. tuberculosis [[58,59\]](#page-11-0). Activation of the INH prodrug depends on the catalase-peroxidase KatG, and katG hypomorphs drive INH resistance in clinical isolates.

Recently, two complementary studies used an impressive suite of methodologies to CS discovery and genetic vulnerabilities mapping in said mutants [[60,61\]](#page-11-0). Per data from the CRISPRi screen, katG hypomorphs are vulnerable to depletion of genes with a function in respiration [\[60\]](#page-11-0). Whereas the effect of repressing the expression of atpD, a subunit of ATP synthase, is bacteriostatic in a drug sensitive (DS) strain, it is bactericidal in a katG mutant.

In congruence, the second study reports CS to bedaquiline (BDQ) in INH-resistant clinical isolates of M. tuberculosis [[61\]](#page-11-0). BDQ is a first-in-class inhibitor of ATP synthase with a bactericidal activity against M. tuberculosis [[62\]](#page-11-0). The authors leveraged on available database to extract antibiotic susceptibility profiles of thousands of clinical isolates [\[63](#page-11-0)]. They noticed a downward shift in the distribution of MIC values to bedaquiline (BDQ) in multidrug-resistant (MDR) isolates; and this sensitization to BDQ of MDR isolates is driven by INH resistance.

As expected, follow-up experiments demonstrated a much higher level of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and oxidative damage in a katG deletion mutant over DS strain in BDQ-treated cultures [[61](#page-11-0)]. However, the CS of katG mutant emanates, in part, by a differential gene expression program at baseline and in response to BDQ. Over-expression of transcription factors Rv3160c and kmtR, both upregulated in katG, is enough to sensitize the DS strain to BDQ [\[61](#page-11-0)].

# Genetic vulnerabilities of acquired antibiotic-resistance genes

Novel genetic dependencies are not limited to de novo resistance mutations in essential and conditionally essential genes. High-level expression of ARGs, too, may adversely affect processes or macromolecules essential for growth [64–[67\]](#page-11-0).

Within the active site of metallo-β-lactamases (MBLs) are two zinc atoms critical for protein folding and carbapenem hydrolysis. A recent study demonstrated that, in low-zinc growth media, expression of the Verona integron-encoded MBL (VIM-2) protracts lag time and slows-down growth rate [\[68](#page-11-0)]. Expression of VIM-2 induces regulons controlling expression of chaperones and proteases across all cell compartments. Dovetailing gene expression data, genetic vulnerability analysis attributes a cost-mitigating effect specifically for the extracytosolic unfolded protein stress response. Cost mitigating genes include degP (encoding a major periplasmic protease) as well as resP and degS (both encoding positive regulators of the σ<sup>E</sup> regulon). Accordingly, VIM-2 expression sensitizes bacteria to small-molecule inhibitors of RseP and DegS [\[68\]](#page-11-0).

Together the results are consistent with a model wherein misfolding of VIM-2 in the periplasmic space, due to low zinc availability, accounts for the growth defect and genetic vulnerabilities. Moreover, extracytosolic unfolded protein stress is known to increase OM permeability to antibiotics. It turns out that VIM-2 expression presents CS to AZI that is driven through enhanced

<span id="page-9-0"></span>![](_page_9_Picture_1.jpeg)

intake across the OM. AZI is normally not indicated for treatment of infections with Gram-negative enterobacterial pathogens such as Klebsiella pneumoniae and Pseudomonas aeruginosa. However, expression of VIM-2 in both pathogens sensitizes them to AZI in a mouse model of infection [[68\]](#page-11-0).

In congruence, a separate study reported CS to AZI and COL in E. coli strains where the gene blaOXA-48 confers resistance to carbapenem [[69\]](#page-11-0). Of note, oxacillinase-48 is not dependent on zinc for its activity, and therefore CS to AZI or COL appears to be a more prevalent liability of carbapenem hydrolyzing enzymes.

# Genetic epistasis and the gene-network supporting phenotypic expression of resistance

Genetic backgrounds that are less compatible with antibiotic resistance are promising on their own [70–[72](#page-11-0)]. Genes that are necessary for expression of the resistance phenotype are considered epistatic to resistance mutations and antibiotic-resistance genes (ARGs) [\(Figure](#page-3-0) 4D,E).

With a curated collection of gene knock-outs in E. coli, an elegant study compared the evolvability of TET resistance across various genetic backgrounds [[70\]](#page-11-0). Independent trajectories frequently evolved TET resistance that is driven by mutation in lon, marR, and acrR [[70\]](#page-11-0). In common to all of them is upregulation of the acrAB efflux system. However, evolution of TET resistance is either curtailed or negated in KOs of dnaK, lpcA, and tolC.

Specifically, no TET resistance could evolve in the background of tolC KO, possibly because it forms complexes with most efflux systems in E. coli. It is interesting to note that TolC is the outer-membrane receptor for certain bacteriophages and colicins, and as such, selection of tolC loss-of-function mutants is possible [[73](#page-11-0)]. However, some tolC mutants may evade this trade-off [\[73](#page-11-0)]. Deep-mutagenesis campaign of tolC reveals phage-resistant mutants without loss of antibiotic efflux activity [\[74](#page-11-0)].

Another study in Acinetobacter baumannii reports epistatic interactions between transposon insertion mutants to blaGES-14 and blaOXA-23, both of which normally confer high-level resistance to CEF and MER [\[71\]](#page-11-0). The authors searched for mutants that are more sensitive to either CEF or MER, and stratified this group based on its response to a combination of antibiotic and the adjuvant avibactam. Transposon mutants indifferent to the adjuvant are candidate epistatic factors important for the activity of β-lactamases. Epistatic factors from this study include dsbAB, likely for the role it has in disulfide bond formation necessary for optimal activity of β-lactamases [[75\]](#page-11-0).

# Concluding remarks

Recent studies have tackled barriers to the future implementation of CS principles in antibiotic therapy. Deep mutagenesis screens of antibiotic binding sites expand coverage and support clustering of resistance mutations and genes in CS and XR studies. They also reveal instances of within-class CS driven by mutually exclusive substrate specificity of binding site mutants. Advanced computational modeling of chemical genetics data further supports CS and XR discovery, overcoming limitations such as low throughput or sensitivity of detection. And screens of natural products and genetically encoded antimicrobials nominate top hits active against resistant strains.
